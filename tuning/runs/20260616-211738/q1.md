## LLM answer

Here's what the literature says on Gurudev's views on bhakti:

> "I will not merely construe texts. I must say what I feel on the point, on my own account. I believe that Bhakti does not consists in religious ceremonials in pilgrimages and in formal idol-worships it consists in love to God and through this love to man... We can never love man so well as when we know that he partakes of the same divine nature which is in us. Love to humanity must be based on love to God. If it is not, it is bound to have a shaky foundation. It is the love which we bear to God that inspires us with love to man and those who love man otherwise love him accidentally and not essentially."
> *— Patankar Pravachan 3, chunk 39 (canonical) · quoting Gurudev's 1912 essay "Vindication of Indian Philosophy"*

**Why this passage:** Gurudev here defines bhakti in its most essential terms — not as ritual observance but as love of God that naturally flows outward as love of man, giving the doctrine a deeply personal and ethical character.

---

> "According to Narada, the highest kind of Bhakti cannot be defined in terms of भरोस. It must be defined in terms of Prema: सा त्वस्मिन् परमप्रेमरूपा... यल्लब्ध्वा पुमान् सिद्धो भवति, अमृतो भवति, तृप्तो भवति।"
> *— Pathway to God in Hindi Literature, chunk 102 (canonical) · Gurudev Ranade*

**Why this passage:** In analysing the Hindi saints, Gurudev foregrounds Narada's definition of supreme bhakti as *parama-prema* — an unconditional, absorbing love for God — favouring this mystical understanding over a faith-based or ceremonial one.

---

> "In their interpretation of the relationship between the Gopis and Krishna, they have not rightly stressed the mystical aspect of the relationship... It is given to women as also to men to have a vision of God, and that is all that the Gopis aimed at, and did achieve in their relationship with Krishna."
> *— Pathway to God in Hindi Literature, chunk 92 (canonical) · Gurudev Ranade*

**Why this passage:** Gurudev insists that the Gopi-Krishna relationship — often taken as the paradigm of bhakti — must be read mystically (as the soul's longing for God-vision) rather than emotionally or sensually, revealing his consistently non-sentimental, spiritually rigorous understanding of bhakti.

---

**Synthesis:** Across his writings, Gurudev's understanding of bhakti is threefold: (1) it is *inward love*, not outward ceremony or pilgrimage; (2) it culminates in *parama-prema* — a total, mystical absorption in God — as articulated by Narada; and (3) it necessarily generates love of fellow human beings, since that love has no stable foundation unless rooted in love of God. Bhakti, for Gurudev, is thus at once the highest mysticism and the deepest ethics.
