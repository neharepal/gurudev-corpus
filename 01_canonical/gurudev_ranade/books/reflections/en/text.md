REFLECTIONS – I
21st February 1912

        To-day I saw a gentleman rebuking a young for a fault, which deserved to be
censured. But the rebuke lost all its force as it was immediately followed by an
‘indiscreet’ confession of a similar fault which the gentleman had committed in his
youth. This confession, though magnanimous, was yet ‘indiscreet’ before the young,
and at that time.

22nd February 1912

       It is the nature of children to be naughty, and it requires a great deal of tact on
the part of the elder to behave properly with them. The best way to behave with
children is never to grow familiar with them: to look to their interests, but never to give
them that freedom, which may one day turn prejudicial to one’s own interests. If
familiarity may be said to breed contempt, the remark is especially true in the case of
children.

      How difficult it is to live with a man, who either does not know manners, or who,
you suppose, does not give as much respect to yourself or to your feelings, as you
would like he should give. The root of the mischief perhaps lies in yourself. You cannot
command respect unless you deserve it!

23rd February 1912

       What a spectacle of temerity man is. It seems as if he has no ballast for his
conduct. His actions are inspired by a supreme consideration of self, with a total regard
for decency or decorum. Moments arise when the whole temerity of the man comes
out, & he is exposed to the ridicule of others. How wonderful it would be if his temerity
could be conquered if not by an actual peep into the future, at least by a complete
confidence in Divine Grace!

       Do not ask God of any favour with all the passionateness of your heart; or, if
you persist in your supplications, the favour will be bestowed on you with a vengeance.
Submit yourself to the Divine Will, and have a complete trust in whatever He does.
Reconcile yourself to the circumstances in which he wishes to place you; and if you
entreat Him passionately to better those circumstances, you do not know whether you
are leaping in the dark. Whatever is ordained is ordained for man’s good!

       Why do people wish that they should be visited by others? If these persons do
not visit them, they take a great deal of offence. There is a great deal of weakness

       Thou laughest at the defects of others; but thou does not know when thou wilt

24th February 1912

       I heard a gentleman to-day descanting on his comparatively poor career in the
University, which, at times, he said made him gloomy. I told him that a man’s worth
was to be judged more from the work in after-life than from the career in the
University. But he said that the work cannot be properly judged, unless one has got
the backbone of a career. A career, he meant to say, touched everything with gold.
One could have a place on the senate, or an examiner working in a University, only by
a career. Was that the only work to be done?

25th February 1912

        At times I wonder how much I talk. Sometimes I reach such a stage while talking,
that it seems painful to break the conversation. Why should we waste our energy in a
talk which is manifestly useless? What is the way out of the habit? Can we not stop
immediately that the idea of garrulity occurs to our mind? It does not matter, if initially,
the break is somewhat painful. Let the break be sudden, & gradually we shall come to
learn how & when to stop.

26th February 1912

      Have you ever met a man, who will refuse to listen to whatever you say, even
though you are right? What is the use of arguing with such a person? As it is hopeless
for you to enlighten your friend, it would be very advisable to break off the
conversation immediately, even at the risk of being called ignorant.

       There are certain persons, with whom a familiarity does breed contempt. Be
very careful before you take such persons in confidence; for every time that you do so,
you are watering the seed of contempt in such persons.

from an audience. How is the applause to be explained? In the first place, the story was
a very probable story; in the second place, there was the principle of a charming

27th February 1912

                You wish that everybody else should subserve to your own self. Why do
you wish that others should read out to you? You had better not aspire for Knowledge,
if it is physically impossible for you to read to yourself. You have tried the experiment
many times, & every time it has failed. Why should you expect that others should play
second fiddle to yourself? Determine, therefore, not to ‘ask’ anybody to do the
slightest favour to you.

        To-day I have got a fourth companion for me in the small room, which was
originally, to all intents & purposes, reserved for me. Why should I grumble at this fate,
if the room does not belong to me? Time was when I chafed even at the time that a
third companion was given me; but now that the fourth has come, I cannot but doubt
that I have been passing through a process of trial. Else why all the things that have
disturbed the peace of my mind for the last month & a half? Why was it that I had to
change from place to place with regard to my meals? Why was it that I had to pass
through two great ordeals of stress and toil? I cannot doubt that it is because there
has been no Harmony within me for a long time, that these things are taking place.

29th February 1912

        What is the art of behaving with a clever, but naughty student? Eccentricity is
supposed to be the mark of genius. Ought that to be so? Can not genius remain
‘genius’ without being eccentric? I know a friend of mine in my own time in college,
who was both intelligent & eccentric. Should we leave genius to its eccentricity,
considering all reforms to be hopeless? Or, should we leave it to time? Or, should we
try to reform it by expostulation & other means?

      Everywhere in the world, you meet with insults, overt or open. This is especially
so, when you have not the power to correct mischief. Should we be guided by the
motive of forbearance, or can there be any other attitude to meet the mischief?

3rd March 1912

      A friend approached M. to-day almost in consternation. M. assured him that he
could not, by any possibility, be led into danger it was worth while making the
demeanour of M. at the time. Could he peep into the future? Or, was his serenity a

7th March 1912

        How do all things lose their sting before calmness! All passions all emotions
seem to die away before tranquillity. A habitual tranquillity is difficult to get. It can’t be
got except by continued & long practice. A plain unassuming look has the power to
kill the transgressions of others, & their insinuations. All other natures look so pale
before an unruffled nature.

8th March 1912

       To-day, I saw a somewhat nude picture lying on my table: I do not know how it
came to be placed there. Possibly it might be the work of some jolly youth. To me, of
course, it was objectionable. But I asked a boy of about 8 years old what he thought
about the picture, & then a boy of fourteen. The first said that the picture was very
good: he looked only to the drawing. The boy of fourteen, however, said that ‘no
objection’ need be taken to that: in him there was a contest between the idea of art,
& the idea of morality.

12th March 1912

        To-day I saw two small stars lying near each other on the sky. They presented a
phenomenon known as ‘double star’. They resembled two minor authors who
collaborate with each other to attract the attention of the public; but a great author is
single: he shines singly like any of the larger stars. We do not see two large stars lying
near each other on the heavenly expanse.

       To-night, the land-lady was wroth that her front door was not shut up before
we retired to bed. The fact was that her own husband was responsible for the door
because it was he who came into the house last. The land-lady did not know this, &
supposed as if we had been responsible for it. She was certainly not to blame, if she
was wroth upon us!

      To-night I saw a physical experiment on the high frequency waves. It was found
that when a lower potential waves be sufficient to kill a man, a higher potential could
not even slightly affect a person. Grief in its low stages would be impossible to bear;
but when it is raised to a pitch, it ceases to torment, if it does not become positive
happiness.

17th March 1912

         How long, Oh God, are you going to keep me in dependence? How long am I
to be fed at other people’s expense? For one reason or other, I have been leading a
life of dependence for the six months past! How long more do you wish me to depend
upon others!

        Is it not real ‘power’ to live in the midst of temptations, and yet not to yield to
them; to live among the “forbidden fruits”, and yet not to be attracted by them? What
is true of fruits literally may be true of fruits metaphysically.

        I saw this morning one very pleasant student. What is it that has inspired in me
a real liking for the person since the very first time that I saw him? He is clever; & yet
he is modest. He is poor, and yet he has a large mind. He is sympathetic, & yet he has
strength. What is it really that produces a liking at first sight? May it be that two such
persons are productive of vibrations that are similar? Or, may those vibrations be
complementary?

        Consider what a minority you are when the people of the world are concerned?
I had been at the Reay Market for voting yesterday, & I sadly came to realise how very
inferior I was when compared with persons of different vocations, & of various grades
in the world.

        How true it is that to think of a passion is to destroy it. I was vexed the other
day by the rudeness of a boy. But when I began to consider that the circumstances in
which he might have been brought up could not but produce rudeness in him, much
of the vigour of my feeling was lessened. The circumstances in which a man is born &
bred, added to the residue of feelings from a previous existence, go to form & fashion
the character of the man. Every act that we are doing in the present, in a similar way,
will influence our future character for good or for ill. And when we understand
character in this light, there is no necessity of growing petulant at the eccentricities of
behaviour.

18th March 1912

       Have you ever seen a person whose chief business is to meddle & to arrogate
to himself all the power he can catch hold of. He would open your door in season &
out of season. He must needs know what you are always doing. He supposes himself
to be the centre from which all power is to radiate! He would glory in the discomfiture
of others, & would suppose he had worked wonders every now and then.

       One does not know what fate is in store for oneself. I learnt to-day that a certain
gentleman in the city had both his son & daughter attacked by plague towards the
very close of the plague season. The family had not come out along with others, and

       Why should I grow nervous with regard to my place of stay, & with regard to
future prospects, when I have once for all determined my life’s occupation? He, in

19th March 1912

       Try to reform yourself before you wish to reform others. You can influence
others more by what you are than you can hope to do by word or deed. When you
have something worth imparting, you need not long for one to come and carry it away.
Such a person or persons must needs come to you, without your hunting after them.
You will, moreover, do more solid work by making yourself moral & spiritual, before
you wish to father your ideas upon others.

        How fondly does man conceive that by a change local, he can obtain a change
internal. Men always ‘hope’ for better days when they suppose they would be able to
put their ideas into practice; and perhaps that time never comes, & they die away
hoping. It is only in the living present, under whatever circumstances or in whatever
position you are, that you can hope to do justice to your ideas. If you let lose the
opportunity in the hope of having a better one, you not only lose the present, but very
likely also lose the future. If you therefore wish something to be done, do it now. Now
or never is the only solution. However little justice you may do to your work just now,
& however unsatisfactory it may look, rely upon it that you done at least ‘something’.

20th March 1912

      It’s wisdoms part to learn when to stop while speaking. Why do you continue
to speak when people show, by their gestures, that they have no relish for the
information you wish to give them? Why do you speak when two people are talking
together, & one of them shows that he has no regard for your opinion, & does not
want you to meddle? Why do you go to a man who shows by his impatience that he
does not perhaps like talking with you for a long time? Leave great men to themselves,
& always be minding your own affairs.

21th March 1912

        I to-day met with an intelligent but vain student. He apparently has no manners,
but seems to be of a strong determination. It was a pleasure to meet this gentleman;
it was more: it was a benefit. For the dull monotony of humble yielding was relieved
to-day by some lively resistance. It is only such persons that teach us the true art of
living with the world. You must recognise the merit of such men, but keep them at an

arm’s length. It is impossible to restrain them, for they will not be restrained; it is
impossible to teach them, for they will not be taught. They show as if they are
conferring a favour upon you, when, as a matter of fact, they are discharging their
duties as students. We certainly must not be humble before such persons: for it might
encourage their vanity. “We must hiss at them, but not bite them”. We must keep them
at an arm’s length, & not allow them to grow familiar with us. You must show as if you
do not care for them, remembering all the while that in the interest of justice their
intelligence per se must be recognised.

       Is there not a limit to one’s gentility? If a servant asks you, for example, to take
away books, which it is his duty to bring, must you yield? You must be kind only within
the limits of decency. Otherwise, every wind that blows will shake you.

      Is it not very ungrateful to ask a gentleman to do some favour to you, and to
deride him at his back even before he has done it? That shows the limits to which
human depravity can descend!

        What a poor show do the so-called ‘great’ men make when you approach them!
Is it not pure waste of time to spend even a few minutes in personal mockery or
indecent talk? True greatness must magnify, & not lessen, in actual presence. A truly
great man is he, who is more respected in presence than in absence.

        How various is thy creation, oh God! At one time, we have to meet with a person,
full of vanity & full of disrespect; immediately afterwards, we meet with another full of
modesty, and full of respect. The very face of the one shows that he is unsympathetic;
that of the other that he is within, to abide by whatever is told him. Presumption, and
indifference are written large in the manner of the one; in that of the other, we see
nothing but a love of knowledge, & a desire to seek. How very difficult it is not to be
repelled by the conduct of the one, & flattered by that of the other!

       It has been considered wisdom’s part to cut jokes at the expense of the persons
or defects of others; & these people are considered popular by the ignorant. Better
not to speak a word, than to speak blasphemy!

23rd March 1912

        Would you prefer scenes of gaiety or of calm tranquillity? What is the good of
being ‘seen’ by others? Why should one wish that one ought to become the load-star
of all eyes? Moreover, is it not fully to sacrifice calm tranquillity for useless pleasure?

         What should be your aim? To please people, or to be just with them? While the
first view looks to the present time, the second looks to the future. By pleasing men
immediately, you may hurt them in the long run; but giving them a just treatment now,
you will really please them in the end. This is the paradox of conduct.

       We had a long talk this day, & we discussed the importance of analytic study.
This kind of study is bound to be slow, as it is concerned with the facts, & not with
glossy theories. It was this kind of study which had given a ‘practical’ importance to
the sciences. Can not philosophy or ethics be made similarly practical, by making a
study & collection of facts? The work is bound to be slow, & to occupy a long time;
but, therefore, it will be more complete, & gradually interesting. Otherwise, we often
find philosophers arguing dogmatically, which a man of science hates. It is neither the
age, nor the learning, nor their speaking or writing power which ought to give
philosophers their importance: they must descend down to the study of sciences, so
that they might cultivate the habit of analytical study & apply it to philosophy.

       Do not talk ill of a man in his absence; for by doing so, when you come into his
presence, you are all the while afraid that he might have known what you had said of
him in absence. Moreover, you must have the courage of dissenting, & telling him to
his very face that you do not approve of such and such a thing. It is not necessary that
you should go forward with a banter, but if the time comes, you might depend upon
yourself to tell him that you do not approve of his conduct.

24th March 1912

        I had been to see the Anniversary Celebrations of the N.E. School. The feature
of the celebration, which struck me most, was the recitation of fine passages in
literature & of beautiful songs by boys not more than 14 years old on the average.
There were two lady-students also who recited two fine passages in English. The first
became a little nervous after a little recitation: the students took advantage of it, and
shouted & clapped their hands. The lady grew pale, & apparently lost the thread of
her recitation. Yet she gathered as much courage as possible, & though her fingers
were shaking, her voice spoke out the further part of the recitation.

       One boy struck me, especially as I was deceived with regard to his qualifications.
I had known the boy for some time past, & ever since I had seen him first, I never knew
that he had a gift of a very beautiful voice. But when I saw this boy bursting out in a
melodious song this afternoon, I was rather surprised that there was much behind the
appearance, which only had been familiar to me.

       To-night I reached a stage in conversation when stopping seemed to be actually
painful. I supposed I had not sufficiently explained myself, & therefore I supposed that
an abrupt stopping would do me injustice; & so, it seemed painful to me. But this
feeling I entirely submerged in reading some fine passages of poetry in B.oham’s
Dictionary of Poetical Quotations. This produced an equanimity of mind, & so I found
myself again easy.

25th March 1912

       Pride is supposed to be the birthright of genius. This was illustrated by one
more example to-day. Two students, intelligent to a certain extent, supposed they were
manifesting their genius in leaving the lecture-hall this afternoon, after they had first
entered it, & sat there for a certain time. I was glad that the class became more tranquil
by their absence; but what of them? Poor creatures, they were putting one more bar
in the way of their evolution – a principle also productive of other kinds of harm in
future. Let me try to find out the proper method of behaviour with such people. Let
me neglect them entirely even when they show forth their genius, remember always
that their merit must nevertheless be duly rewarded.

28th March 1912

         If other people behave with you in a manner which shows their indifference
towards yourself, you had better not care much for these people. You should never
push yourself before their notice, simply because they are great. And, are they really
great that is the question. Do they turn every moment of their life to use? And does
not true greatness consist in being perennially engaged? Why waste our time on
trifles? How much more important it is to spend one’s time in self-introspection, if any
be at hand! It does not matter if you abruptly leave company in the interest of work.
What is the use of wasting day after day in some trifle which one does not anticipate
beforehand, but which nevertheless comes surely. Days over days of this term have
been passed this way – without self-introspection, without the increase of potential
sympathy & virtue. I must determine to turn every moment of my time to advantage.

       How careless have I grown! The other day I lost Tukaram’s Gatha while alighting
at the station, simply because I did not remember to take it with me. To-day, I lost
Stout’s Groundwork of Psychology while alighting in Shukrawar. I must try and reform
my carelessness.

         Mr. Jayakar told us yesterday one very important thing. He asked us to discover
the latent power within ourselves. By self-observation, and from the true report of our
well-wishers & friends, & perhaps from the criticisms of non-sympathisers, we might
gather the latent virtue in us. We might then nourish it day after day. We must feed it
a little. If we write English well, we must devote more time to the cultivation of style by
a study of great authors; if we learn that we have a latent power of speaking, we must
study that art. We do not want mediocrities: we are sick of them. What we want is
perfection & specialisation.

       I realised to-day how solitude helps the power of making speeches. Art can
achieve a good deal, though it cannot equal Nature. A man may not be born an orator;
but he can make himself a good speaker if he tries. The Hon. Mr. Gokhale is reported

to have committed passages after passages to memory & to have reproduced them.
Would it not be worth while, in the interest of learning to speak or write well, to commit
to memory many famous & select passages? And would not the continued practice of
speaking or writing to oneself help one in making a good speaker or writer?

       I saw two people growing nervous before a large assembly to-day & yesterday.
The chief fault was with the vocabulary, and their want of practice. If one supposes one
is almost becoming non-phrased, & a word or a phrase is not coming on the lip, one
had better begin the next sentence, without waiting for a word or phrase which does
not suggest itself. We must, moreover, have boldness of face, & ought not to show
that we are growing nervous.

       Every little favour shown to you makes you proportionately indebted to the
benefactor. So care ought to be taken before receiving favours. If possible, you must
not put yourself in a position, which would excite the sympathy of others, and therefore
cause them to help you.

29th March 1912

        What a dull day I passed to-day without any intellectual illumination, & without
any tranquil thought! I slept more than necessary last night, & the result was that I got
from my bed gloomy & morose. I must undertake measures to reduce the period of
sleep. I must determine to get up as soon as the period of the ‘first sleep’ is over. I
must try the experiment of reducing the quantity of food at the time of my dinner, &
must see what the result would be.

2nd April 1912

       I have been in quite an intolerable sloth lately. I do not suppose that there are
any higher responsibilities than merely eating, drinking, sleeping, & doing college
work. Have I not to do some work for my own development? Have I not perhaps to
appear for the M.A. sometime or other? And what preparations have I been making
for the same.

       I had been yesterday to preside at the H.J. Festivals at the Deccan College. On
the one hand, I was struck with the indifference of the man who had invited me, & on
the other, by the disinterested work of a junior B.A. student, who seem to love the
celebrations! I was at least glad that the enthusiasm of the latter made up for any
deficiency of the celebrations.

       Time was when in my college career I used to rise up to address the students at
the D. College, & I was always received with a general ovation. But did I not find
yesterday that very few had cared to attend the meeting, and fewer to applaud? Time
doth thus work wonders.

       I was sorry to find this morning one innocent student charged by his friends
with having told a lie. The fact was that the student under consideration was entirely
innocent of the affair, & his friends, without any due consideration, & with an
haphazard suspicion, found fault with the innocent fellow. Does this not show that
when we charge others with any fault, we must have ample evidence to justify our
suspicion?

       I met this afternoon a friend of mine, who told me how a man come to be
domineered over by others. Any little benefaction which they confer upon him makes
him indebted, & therefore servile, to them that confer the favour on him. Even under
such circumstances only a strong will & a strong conviction will serve to guide the man
out of the difficulty. Can not the benefaction be returned?

       Do you know how your own friends behave with one another? Do they all like
each other? And as a central personality, what should you do that they may stick fast,
& never grow either jealous of each other, or have a low opinion about each other?
The fault lies in you that you do not find out the way that they may live with each other
peacefully. Or, you can live in ignorance, or superior disdain, of their differences.

      People really do attach importance to practice as a justification of precept. A
young student need not advise his elder friends on a matter, in which he himself has
not made any great advancement.

3rd April 1912

        How wonderful it is that when a man is placed in the midst of adversity, he is
inclined to believe in divine justice; but no sooner is he in a comparatively better
position, every little thing which may deprive him of it seems to be an act of Divine
injustice. Whenever a man begins to suppose that not all the things that are ordained
from above are for his good, whenever his belief in divine justice is shaken, put it down
that he is involving instead of evolving, degenerating instead of making progress! God
grant that we might ever look upon whatever is as right: let not our faith in divine
justice be shaken to the smallest extent. Even if we are passing through a series of
misfortunes, let us consider it as a process of trial; let us suppose that we are being
tried, & let us not therefore be found wanting.

their fellows, who looked sorry. But as events turned out, the former went & perished

10th May 1912

        In prosperity, we ought to make use of the principles which we have learnt in
adversity. God sends both prosperity & adversity as a sort of trial upon us; and
powerful is he who fashions his conduct in prosperity according to the lessons he has
learnt in adversity. One realises the truth of the statement that in prosperity, there are
friends in plenty, but in adversity, not one in twenty. We do actually experience “life’s
chequered scenes of joy and sorrow”. The very same people who had waited upon us
in our original condition of prosperity had frowned on us in adversity; but as soon as
we have again reached a condition of prosperity, what will be the attitude of the
people?

        The sum of happiness in the world is constant, as Spinoza says. The joy which
one feels at a sudden turn of fortune exactly counterbalances the pain which others
feel at a corresponding evil turn. The two together are always constant. Where then is
the cause of elation?

      If you habituate ourselves to a constant equanimity of mind no good or bad
news can ruffle you. People will rather be astonished to find that you do not look upon
them in the light in which you might be expected by them to do. That is real power
when no good news is able to shake you.

      And in fact, is there anything as good news? The thing which you consider good
might turn out to be bad in the long end. Addison implored God to give him only
those things which He considered good: should we not in a similar strain always
implore God to keep us in a condition which He ultimately considers good for us
according to His own ends?

6th June 1912

        It is very ungentlemanly on the part of one of the audience to try to prevent the

26th June 1912

         A hill looks beautiful from a distance, says a Marathi proverb. Similarly, a good
& rich position looks charming from a distance. Just after arriving at it, you become
convinced that it throws greater responsibilities on yourself: people expect more from
you. Moreover, it is attended with the same kinds of ills, with which a lower position
might be. There are the same physical discomforts: there is the same, if not more,
liability to ill health & disease; there are the same, if not stronger, moral temptations.
And if we fall we fall from a greater height, & therefore the position is risky. Moreover,
there is the possibility of one’s being at once deprived of the proud position – and
then?

        Remember that the correspondence of another is a ‘possession’ of him; hence
it is inviolable. A man who reads the correspondence of another even unwillingly is
committing a moral sin. Correspondence, like woman or wealth, must always be
considered inviolable.

       Where is the necessity of being introduced to newer men, when those who are
already acquainted with you have ceased to be of you? I do not deny that there is
possibility of finding good men among the new people; but why seek acquaintance?
If men are good, & if they are of you, they will sometime come to you; if they are not
of you, the less you know them, the better. Good people, it may be said, must
sometimes meet & love each other, & work together. Otherwise by hunting after new
men, we might catch Tartars.

       When people have once ceased to sympathise with your opinions, the less
connection you have with them either by correspondence, or meetings, or
conversation, or discussion, or co-operation, by feasting together or by living together,
the better it is for your own tranquillity.

       Oh! What a fall was there! A religious preacher, who had brought under his
influence hundreds of people, has at last came to ruin owing to moral guilt. His
disciples have discarded him; his place has been deserted; & his work is being
demolished. Oh, the short sighted folly of man that passions should get the better of
reason. What a touching scene that – when the faithfullest of men have deserted him
– men of strong purpose, of extreme devotion to work, & full of sacrifice!

of youth, people do not care for regular work; but when they become aware of the

command the original energy. Be most regular & sharp in your duties. It is here more
than anywhere else that the truth of the maxim is very keenly felt: “do not put off till
to-morrow what you can do to-day”. Do not engage in idle talk; and do not allow other
persons to draw you in a useless & time-killing talk. Many a little maketh a miracle,

        Nature gives us moral lessons in her everyday acts. A young parrot was almost
half-killed by a cat; but it was released by a person hard by, & put into a cage for
safety’s sake. Now the mother of the parrot saw this, & even though her young was in
a human cage, she used to come daily & feed the young one until it came of age.
Parental love is thus illustrated by the instance of an insignificant, & perhaps, an
irrational bird. What made the mother to come & feed her young one? – instinct or
reason?

        As I was going to take my bath this morning, I saw the whole atmosphere
surcharged by rain-flies. Last night it rained somewhat heavily, & these creatures
possibly owned their sudden appearance to yesterday’s rain. As I began to observe
them; they began to cast off their wings, & ceased to fly. Two such creatures generally
linked together & one of them closely followed the other on the surface of the field.
Wherever they went together, perhaps no harm was done to them; but wherever there
was a single one, a large black ant came, attacked it; killed it, & carried it away possibly
as trophy, certainly as prey. Within half an hour the whole field seemed covered with
the wings of these creatures, & there was almost no fly left which could fly. Here &
there, an un-winged creature was seen moving on the earth, but it seemed as if the
whole race of these creatures had died away within half an hour. How very momentary
the life of these creatures; how very harmless, & therefore how very open to attack!
The weaker must go to the wall, and there is no place for them in Nature’s economy.
The strong, the powerful, possibly the ruffian, creatures must succeed. It is easy to
explain why meekness is not rewarded in Nature!

2nd July 1912

      Then are you most responsible, when you suppose that there is nobody to
watch you. Then is the lesson of duty rightly learnt when you do it without reference
to ends, or without fear of examination. Duty itself watches you, when you suppose
you are responsible to none, & if you neglect your work, you sin in the eyes of Duty,
& therefore of God. God loves the man who discharges his duties regularly &
enthusiastically; and such a man has no cause to be afraid of anybody, when the time
comes when his work is put to the test.

        But here, as elsewhere; there is the danger of sacrificing oneself to work. The
work should not be allowed to suppress the man. As we owe a duty to our master for
the remuneration he gives, so we owe a duty to God for the life he has put in us. The
first should not be allowed to come in the way of the second, far less to suppress it.
Work while you work; but meditate when you ought to meditate.

8th July 1912

        This same gentleman entertained the students of this college by his brilliant
poetical feats this evening. One of the greatest feats he achieved was to compose three
lines of verse before he ever knew the fourth line, which anybody specified might have
in mind in such a way that the three lines might have a perfect connection with the

        People are, as a rule, desirous to know from others how they themselves
perform the part entrusted to them. If a man speaks, he must needs expect his friends
to say out whether they liked his speech or not. If a man writes, he must have the
satisfaction of learning from others how he fared. So with regard to singing, or the
composition of verse. This is an idol of the tribe, & can be subdued only by being
entirely indifferent to success. If it is necessary, from a higher standpoint, that a man
should write or speak well, why he is bound to be a good writer or speaker without
concerning himself with it.

lead them to gape, & feel astonished, but it will not lead them to analyse the affair, so
as to be able to reproduce the conditions. Students were merely merry when they
witnessed to-day’s poetical feat: none perhaps cared to analyse. A desire or capacity
for analysis comes only after a long experience, or thought.

         It’s a long time since I wrote last in this note-book. Yesterday I finished my 26th
year according to the Hindu calendar. What can I find on a review of the life which has
been already passed? Does it show certain germs which might fructify in time to come?

and spirituality. How proud does a man become when he has a position for himself, &
how very regardless of other men’s convenience! It is only when he sees himself slightly
treated by other people like himself, people who have an equal or better position in
life, that his eyes are opened & then he begins to care for the feelings of others.

experiences should come in the heels of one another in order that if the wand be bent
to one extreme at one moment, it might be immediately bent to the other at the very
next. The mind also might fair remain indifferent to either joy or sorrow!

24th July 1912

        On Sunday last, the 21st instant, it rained very heavily in Poona. The rainfall on
that day was 4 inches & 36 cents. one of the record rainfalls of Poona during the period
of a day. That was the first appreciable rain received in Poona this year. Ever since the
Monsoon began, & as it began to advance without a drop of rain, fears had begun to
be entertained that this year also there was to be a famine; & as it came on the heels
of the draught of the last year, it seemed very probable that this would really be a very
fearful year especially for the poor & the cattle. But soon the tables turned, & in three
days’ time, Poona experienced something like 7 inches of rain, culminating, of course,
on the Sunday mentioned. It rained very fearfully on Sunday night; it seemed as if the
river would enter in its floods in the heart of the city. The fears were partly realised; the
floods had reached the Shanwar Maruti, & Prof. Dixit’s house was in water.
Omkareshwar was deep under water. The floods also entered the Wadi near Lakadipul
and turned people out of doors into the open space. The Bund Garden registered a
height almost equal to that reached in 1896, & the dam & the lower level could hardly
be distinguished from each other. A field on the road on this side of the Boat Club was
under water; the road to the Sappers & Miners from behind the College was under
water, & many poor people from the Wadi were left desolate in the open space behind
the College or the College stables, suffering along with children, cattle, dogs & old
women from bitter wind & sharp showers of rain. The Holkar bridge was entirely
submerged under water, & its railing was swept away. Thus did the prospects of a
bitter draught change in three days’ time to a prospect of prosperity! Thousands of
people & students from other colleges & schools had been wandering that day having
a look at the floods, which were merely a handiwork of God. One wonders how they
would have felt if they had seen God himself manifested in all his glory, sublimity &
power!

deserve it. “It’s not sufficient to command respect; we will do more, we will deserve it”.

24th July 1912

       There is a great truth in the saying that a thing can be judged best from the
outside. I could judge of the D. College while I was at the Fergusson. I can now judge
of the Fergusson when I am in this college. What is true of a building is true also of
any subject; you can’t judge of it when you are in it. Just as we must go out of a word
to know its expanse or circuit, so we must go out of any subject to judge of its scope.
One can judge India better when one is in Germany, & one’s own philosophy better
when one goes to the philosophy of other countries.

       We read in books that we should act in such a way that each to-morrow might
find us further than to-day! But do we do this? Do we examine whether we have grown
any the wiser by having spent each “to-day”? Do we add unto our spirituality? Do we
examine what is the net progress of every day before we retire to bed? If we do not,
where is the necessity of sleeping? Why should we not first add something to
ourselves, before we recline on our happy bed? We should study if we have not already
studied, see whether a moral lesson has been added onto us in the days experience &
examine what we have done to advance our spirituality. A day spent is so much as a
day cut off from our life. We shall live one day less. We should counterbalance this
physical disability by, intellectual, moral, or spiritual gain every day.

25th July 1912

appreciated only by those who have tried to bring themselves nearer to the ideal. It is
a great mental asset to have the power of meeting opposites with the same mental
attitude. As generally happens, we do feel flattered by compliments, & sorry at epithets
which do not come up to the honour which we would fain wish to have. It is always
best in the interest of mental peace to think, at any particular moment, of the opposite
experience to that which one is enjoying. Another method is to meet both the opposite
experiences with a stern attitude at the particular moments. Sternness has the power
of softening rough experiences, & of nullifying the intoxicating ones, for it must be
admitted, that in both kinds of experiences, the nerves are undergone a peculiar strain,
which is far from bringing on the joy of tranquillity.

30th July 1912

       As one observes the work, one sees that it is nothing but a contest of passions:
one man tries to gain as much dignity for himself as possible; the other tries to prevent
him from doing so. The first tries to raise himself to the dignity of the second; the
second tries to keep the first as much low as he possibly can. The first is glad when he

second is sorry that the first was given the opportunity. Here we see, better than

happiness tends merely to produce sorrow in another. The philosopher is amused to

causes, & to observe the working, of the passions is a sure way to destroy them &
whenever we are prompted to be affected by any passion, we must immediately

       People appropriate to themselves great capability of thought & strength of
conviction, but if you examine them, you will find their thoughts childish, & their
convictions flexible. They do not know that the simplest thought is always the best and
also the strongest. But one must bear even with such people that they might one day
be convinced of their own petty and weak thoughts.

      There is some magnetic influence in the deportment of many men. But one
must examine whether it is deservedly so or not. Things which attract, & yet are not
worthy, must be left to take care of themselves; but those which attract & are worthy
must be cherished with love.

        There are certain people whose very look seems to be repulsive on account of
their ingrained thoughts & habits. Such persons must not disturb oneself. It is ever
good never to speak or hear about such people, or to dilate upon their faults in
discussion. The less that is said or heard about them, the better for one’s peace.

15th September 1912

       And yet how true is that such people are but the product of their circumstances
& actions. An invariable law governs vice & its result, & when we look at the Law by
which these people have come to be repulsive, they cease any longer to respect us.

        How much does accident governs our life? The Titanic clashed with an iceberg
& was wrecked. Other ships which were not far off were saved. What was this due to
but to accident? A man goes to a place where the first footstep he places brings to
light a scorpion, & so he returns. In a moment’s time, another goes there without the
knowledge that there is a scorpion, & the scorpion has run off, & does not come in his
way!

      One becomes occasionally reminded how so much of the world is guided by
worship of the Dollar. Is not a rich man flattered for his riches, & a lucky man for his
luck?

       Beware of a man who would say evil of you at your back, & keep his
countenance before you. He would laugh at you, because you did not squander
money, which he wanted you to do. It does not matter in the least if you incur
displeasure of others by not wasting your money: mind only to spend it on good
causes.

15th October 1912

       It is very true that an unreal thing may produce a real effect. A poet tells a story,
perhaps fashioning it out of his own mind, there being possibly no historical
foundation for it. It is a very pathetic story, & it serves in spite of its historical unreality,
to bring forth tears from the eyes of a very sentimental person, simply because it is
poetically real. The difference between real & unreal in such a case is extremely
delicate. Historic unreality & poetic reality may exist together & may serve to produce
quite a real effect.

       I was struck, not to a small extent, by such a story causing tears in the eyes of a
great man, & choking his throat, & thus making unable even to read. What would you
say of such a sentimentality? You might perhaps say “weakness”; yes, & I might add
“strength”. Such a person alone is capable of finer emotions. He is the type of a man,
who is fit to become a “sick soul” as James would call him. Such a person can evolve
himself on the lines of Devotion rather than on those of thought.

      A double impossibility is conveyed in the idea of a deaf man hearing the
words of a dumb man, or of a blind man seeing a lame man running!
