Opportunities of College Life
                       Dr. R. D. Ranade

It is matter of common observation that at every change of
season, the whole body of nature is disturbed from its usual
equillibrium. The natural forces are then in council and the state
of Nature seems to be undergoing a little insurrection. Similar is
the case when man is passing from one stage of his life to
another. Perhaps the critical of these changes occur when
boyhood is making room for manhood, when imagination is
superseded by reason. And as these changes generally occur
when a man is passing from a school life to college life, college
life has come to be the most critical portion of a man's life, for it
is here that he can be said to be the architect of his own future
in the truest sense. He is being introduced in the world from the
cloister of his home life and he is brought under the influence of
new friends and new ideas. The school life which he fancied
was full of restraint, is now over, and he fondly conceives that
the new life may now invlove any responsibilities.

But here he is entirely mistaken. If he thinks a little he will come
to know that it is impossible to enjoy college life twice, that
there is tide in his affairs which taken at the flood will lead him
on to the future. Man's life instead of seeming a bed of roses
will appear to him full of duties, both to himself and to Society,
and so he will determine to make such preparations in college
as will enable him to discharge when he comes to begin the
world, his respective duties to himself and society. He
determines to make the most of the opportunities afforded to
him by his college life; and he begins to look upon this period
as a distinct preparation for a life to come.
And so he sets himself to the wheel: The very first thing which
strikes him in the new kind of life, is the unique combination of
individual and corporate life. He not only exists for himself, but
also exists for the society in which he moves. A double
responsibility of cultivating-individual and social virtues thus
fixes itself upon him. He must never be entirely self regarding;
his college and co-mates must equally usurp his attention. In
his clubs, in his games, in his studies and in his quarters, he is
always surrounded by a hazy multitude, of which he has no
accurate conception for a long time to come. He does not know
his duties and he is almost always bewildered in the begining.
But time goes on, and mist begins to be clear; he begins to
learn that he is an integral part of the society which surrounds
him. He has an equal right to manage the affairs of his club,
and of his gymkhana. He can take part in the debating union, or
write in his college magazine with as much liberty and
prospects as any other man. He is inspired by a virtuous
emulation, and his spirits rise within him. "What man has done,
man may do" and so he begins his endeavours.

But it would be a mistake on his part if he supposes that college
life gives him opportunities only for debating or for writing or
even for reading a whole library of books. He must cultivate a
real love for knowledge for his province." He must begin to take
'pleasure' in some kind of study, whether it be literal, scientific,
or professional; for pleasure alone is the mark of progress.
Whether he may or may not agree in supposing that the 'proper
study for mankind is man." Whether he may take an equal
interest in the study of the plant, the rock or the animal or even
if he presumes to see God, the only citerion of his progress
would be the intensity of pleasure which he feels in the object
of his study. So he must make pleasure the only true test of his
love of knowledge. And if he begin to inquire into the origin of
this kind of pleasure he will find that it is the direct outcome of
an equanimity of mind produced in the process of thought, and
so he will begin to think of strengthening his power of thought.

But neither by cultivating a real love of knowledge, nor by
strengthening the power of thought, not even by devoting
attention to physical culture without which by the by intellectual
development would be lame, not even by a combination of
these, will he find that his education is complete. Intellectual
knowledge without physical culture, would be really flame; but
intellectual knowledge without moral development would be
dangerous. It is for the formation of the character that college
life gives a student most excellent opportunities. He has no
doubt learnt rudimentary moral principles at school, but he has
to cultivate the higher virtues. It is taken for granted that he has
learnt to be regular and obedient in many cases even
persevering. But he must now cultivalte the nobler qualities of
admiration and sympathy, of humanity and chastity. He must
begin to admire what is noble and good in others; for in this
way he can increase his potentiality of producing a likeness of
the thing admired. He has excellent opportunities of doing so;
he comes in contact with men, much more learned than he is.
He can if he chooses, admire their learning and morality and so
have an opportunity of producing a likeness of those qualities in
himself. He must have sympathy with others; this virtue can be
easily cultivated as he comes in contact with so many men of
different opinions and denominations. He must cultivate
humility by putting before models of persons exhibiting a unique
and perhaps a rare combination of learning and modesty. And
last and perhaps the most important, he must cultivate chastity
in action and in speech and most pricipally in thought. He must
always have a choice and good company and college affords
him if he likes, splendid opportunities for forming lifelong
friendships. He must cultivate good habits, by a continuous and
recurring performance of good acts by a double process of
observation, observation of others, and observation of himself,
he can become aware of the faults and remedy them. This last,
namely, self-observation, is most fruitful but we must hurry on
with its bare mention lest this be a moral homily.

But another great responsibility rests upon the student, there
must be some backbone to his morality, and so he must try to
base his morality on religion. We have seen before, that
intellectual education without moral would be dangerous,
but moral education without religion would be both
groundless and insipid. A student must therefore try to derive
moral sanction from his religion, otherwise his morality would
be utilitarian. This necessity of religious training, important for
students of any country is appealingly so in the case of Indian
student. By the very necessity of the case, education in India
was founded by Lord Macaulay on non-religious lines and this
system has made secular education so easy and extensive in
India, though unfortunately his motives were not above suspen.
The       existing     educational    bodies      are    therefore
undenominational; and it is perhaps beneficial that they are so.
But nevertheless a great responsibility rests upon students
individually to fill up the deficiency of the system. The great
work before the educated students in future, would be to
rationlize their religion (without however the usual cannotation
of the term) to believe only so far as they know to make
strenuous efforts for this knowledge. No amount of
philosophical and religious reading would make a man
religious; religion knows no language, but that of heart. It is only
in religious feeling that religious education consists; and this
cannot be taught by book or men. College life is greatly suited
for such education if one only tries strenuously. The pure life
which a student finds around himself, the nobility of example,
the opportunity for religious thought, "the starry heavens above
and the moral law within" might all incline him if he chooses to
have religious feeling and that is the essential fact of religious
instruction.

There is one important point which deserves a special
consideration. We have seen above that the true idea of
college life consists in taking it to be a continuous preparation
for the life so come. This indirectly implies that the life work, if
possible, should be determined in college life or rather the
college life should be a continuous struggle to find out for what
work one is destined. If we wish to make a literary career for
ourselves, we must sow its seeds while yet we are in college; if
we wish to devote our life to science, we must cultivate an
ardent love for the subject; if we wish to step on the slippery
path of politics, we must take great pains to observe the
execution of constitutions in Ancient and Modern history; if
again we wish to spend our lives in the service of society and to
make the human lot a better one, from the religious or moral
point of view, we can cultivate the moral virtues by a life of
renunciation; when we have thus determined our goal, when
we are making strenuous efforts to prepare ourselves for it
when we have attained to a high state of perfection by physical
and intellectual, moral and religious culture, let no sceptic say
that we have wasted our college life in vain.
