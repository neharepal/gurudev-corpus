8- Prof. W. D. S. Brown

“The Indian Philosophical Review is the name of a new quarterly, the first number of which has been sent us for review. It is edited by Alban G. Widgery and R. D. Ranade and is published by the Oxford University Press, Bombay. Its appearance is most attractive, and its 95 pages of clear type contain some excellent matter. A most suggestive article is that by Prof. A. G. Widgery on Philosophy and Life.

Another excellent article is “Sankaracharya’s Criterion of Truth,” in which Pandit Mahabhagvat of Kurtkoti shews that this famous sage upheld the value of revelation, as through the Vedas, as well as the use of reason in its interpretation. “Psychology in the Upanishads,” by Prof. R. D. Ranade, brings to light the great variety of view to be found in these writings, for instance, on the question of sleep. Other subjects dealt with are Artistic Production, Jainism, Hindu Law, Zarathustra, etc. The number opens with Minutes of Proceedings of the Indian Philosophical Association, of which this magazine is the organ, and the combined enterprise seems full of promise.”

(The Theosophist magazine Edited by Annie Besant, Vol XXXIX October 1917, Pg. 115)

9- Sir Herbert Stanley Reed

Editor, The Times of India  (1907–1924), A British Politician

“The appearance of this (The Indian Philosophical Review, Edited by Prof. A. G. Widgery and Prof. R. D. Ranade) new Review makes us wonder why something was not done earlier in this direction to meet the needs of serious students of Philosophy in India. That there is an urgent demand and wide scope for such a periodical no one can doubt who has read the short account of the Association given in the current number. We trust that the Review will reach the same high standard as Mind (a British Journal) has always maintained,…

The scope of the Review is to be as broad as philosophy in the widest technical sense of that term. But an endeavour will be made to encourage scholarly rather than a popular treatment, and it is hardly necessary to mention that special attention will be given to Eastern, particularly, Indian Philosophy….By being a medium for comparison of philosophical ideas of the East and the West, the Review will not only do much to restore the ancient glory of India in the region of Philosophy, but will also tend to focus the attention of the great thinkers of the West on the Indian speculative thought, with the result that the Western Philosophy as well as Eastern will be quickened and benefited thereby ……..We trust that the Review will soon take its place among the great Philosophical Magazines, and give a much-needed impetus to philosophical pursuits in India.”

(The Times of India, 26th September 1917)

10- The Kalpaka

India’s only Psychic and Spiritual Review, Tinnevelly (Tamil Nadu)

“Professor R. D. Ranade in a series of three articles in the Indian Philosophical Review makes a systematic survey of all the main points of psychology in the Upanishads such as has never before been attempted. All the passages in the Upanishads on which the series of Upanishadic Psychology is based are carefully collected and printed with accurate reference at the end of each article under the heading ‘Sources’. He considers the subject under three broad heads Empirical Psychology, Abnormal Psychology, and Rational Psychology: the first two divisions being disposed of in a single article.

In the domain of Empirical Psychology, the dexterity with which Prof. Ranade has used his materials becomes clear from the way in which he extracts a psychology of ‘fear’ from passages of manifestly metaphysical import, and brings out clearly the persistent antagonism between Voluntarism and Intellectualism as seen in the Upanishads. In Abnormal Psychology, Prof. Ranade brings out the importance attached in the Upanishads to discourses on the nature of Death, and enters into a fairly exhaustive survey of the ancient views on the nature of Sleep and the Dream-state.

The sections on Rational Psychology open with a profound criticism of Modern Psychology which has substituted a Self as a centre of interest in place of the ancient conception of the Soul.

The last article and the second on Rational Psychology starts with a very sound criticism of the view that the idea of Transmigration is a late non-Aryan intruder into Indian Philosophy. Prof. Ranade has in three papers on Psychology in the Upanishads done a great deal to secure a correct interpretation of ancient Indian thought in one of its most important aspects.”

(May 1918)
