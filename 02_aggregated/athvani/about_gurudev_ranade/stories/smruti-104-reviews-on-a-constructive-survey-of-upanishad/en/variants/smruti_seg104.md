32- The Kesari

“It is a matter of pride to the whole of Maharashtra that an intellectual architect like Prof. Ranade should have created this stately edifice, in which are set jewels and pearls, and diamonds, and rubies, so as to attract even the listless gaze of a casual observer. It will no doubt redound to spread his fame beyond the confines of India into the whole world.

Without meaning any disparagement of the great work of Prof. Deussen, we can say confidently that Prof. Ranade’s work has undoubtedly gone beyond Deussen’s. The author has also shown marvellous skill in suggesting his own point of view through a developmental presentation of Upanishadic ideas.”

(Poona, November 1926)

33- Journal of Indian History

“The author (Prof. R. D. Ranade) is already well known as a Sanskrit Scholar in the department of Philosophy, and the treatise under review is worthy of his eminence and scholarship. He has brought to bear upon the subject his vast knowledge, the historical method of comparative religion, and the evolution of spiritual ideas and methods of realization. He approaches the subject from a universal, and not from any sectarian and narrow point of view….The book supplies a decided want, and it is the best book on the philosophy of the Upanishads that has so far appeared.”

(Madras, December 1926)

38- Arthur B. Keith

Professor of Sanskrit, University of Edinburgh

“The author ingeniously completes his scheme by pointing out that in the Bhagavadgita we find the doctrine of duty for its own sake, which is necessary to supplement the deficiencies of the ethical doctrines of the Upanishads themselves. Not the least interesting section of his work is chapter IV in which he explains his views of the development of later philosophies from the Upanishads; the case made out for Sankara's doctrine as a legitimate development is moderate and reasonable. The adduction after each chapter of the Sources relied on is a convenience, and the book forms a distinctly useful addition to the already large literature on the Upanishads.”

(The School of Oriental Studies, London 1927)

39- Walter Ruben

Director of Institute of Indian Studies, Humboldt University, Berlin

“Dr. Ranade’s Constructive Survey is intended as a supplement to the editing of the Upanishads (the creative period of Indian Philosophy 1927). The most interesting thing about the second chapter is that Dr. Ranade uses the Greek natural philosophy of the pre-Socratics for comparison. It is a nice find by Dr. Ranade about the similarities in the definition of the Ionian element of Aristotle with that of the brahman in Taittiriya Upanishads. It is admitted that the Upanishads are collections of fragments of various thinkers of different times and regions, but the unity of the Upanishad and its lofty height are now sought to be a compendium of all philosophy. As little as this method has anything to do with European philosophy, it is so cleverly executed, and those who study Indian philosophy will have to reckon with the name Ranade, as with the name Radhakrishnan.

A critical reader can draw lots of inspiration from Ranade's book.”

(Translated from original German Review to English)

(Orientalist Literature 1931, No. 1)

40- The New Age

A British Literary Magazine

“This is a first rate publication on the philosophy of the Upanishads by an Indian scholar of great merit. The book is divided into seven chapters covering a wide range of subjects, including the Cosmogony, the Psychology, the Metaphysics and the Ethics of the Upanishads. It starts with the background of the Upanishadic speculation, and finishes with intimations of Self-realization. Roots of later philosophies are also traced in the mass of Upanishadic speculation.

The treatment throughout is thorough, systematic, and impersonal: and the Scriptures of the Hindus are ably interpreted in a terminology widely familiar to students of philosophy in the West. The author's intimate knowledge of ancient Greek thought and modern European philosophy has led him to point out some striking resemblances between Upanishads and Western thought. A Hindu's interpretation of his own scriptures carries with it a reality peculiarly intimate just as much as a Christian's reading of his Bible does. The book is an excellent and substantial production.”

(London, January 20, 1927)
