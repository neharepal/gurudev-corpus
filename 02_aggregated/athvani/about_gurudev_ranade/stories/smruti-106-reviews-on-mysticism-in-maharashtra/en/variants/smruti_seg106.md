Reviews on the ‘Mysticism in Maharashtra’ (1933)

45- Verrier Elwin

The Chief of the Anthropological Survey of India

A British self-trained Anthropologist, Ethnologist and Tribal activist

“The biographies, spiritual experiences and teachings of these mystics are described and analysed by Prof. Ranade with a wealth of knowledge and with deep insight. It is far too encyclopaedic to bear summary and I must be content to say that this book is by far the most readable, interesting and significant of the histories of Indian Mysticism and is obviously written from the author’s heart. Prof. Ranade’s translations are the best thing in his book.

It is significant because it is an attempt by a Hindu scholar to display his own treasures in a Western tongue and to set them in some relation to Western thought.

I commend it, especially to Western readers.”                                                            (The C.S.S. Review, Poona, December 1933)

46- Prof. S. S. Suryanarayana Sastri

Head of Department of Philosophy, University of Madras

“The novelty of the field covered- Maharashtra Mysticism- is a primary attraction, and the interest thus aroused is fully sustained by the fullness and philosophical ability of the presentation. Prof. Ranade uses no rhetoric, but he succeeds eminently, by the very simplicity of his presentation, in making us live and feel with the mystics whom he writes about. One of his most successful chapters thus is that about the contemporaries of Namdeva,….. He makes these figures live by his very artlessness which is an art.”

(The Indian Review, Madras, November, 1933)

47- Dr. Ganganath Jha

Vice-Chancellor, University of Allahabad

“The world of scholars is much beholden to the talented writer Ranade....The volume just to hand deals with the much misunderstood subject of mysticism. It has been a queer irony of fate that there has been a lot of mist surrounding the transparent simplicity of mysticism, from time immemorial, it has been regarded as allied to indeed, at times, even identical with occultism.

We have before us a work of unique importance, in fact, it will be no exaggeration….. to say that we have here as clear an exposition as is possible of mysticism, which, by its very nature baffles explanation; and by a careful perusal of the book, no one can remain in doubt as to what the mysticism of the famous Maharashtra saints was, and in what way every one of us can benefit by their teachings. The work is fully documented and the original sources have been all duly indicated. This plan, is so religiously followed by the talented writer, has saved him from the temptation of fastening his own ideas on the older writers, a Temptation which very few of our modern writers have been able to withstand.”

(The Leader, February 22, 1933)

48- I. B. Horner

An English Indologist, President of the Pali Text Society

“Prof. Ranade presents to us in this work not only critical biographies, but, more especially, full and revealing expositions of the spiritual insight and experiences of five great Indian mystics. Their dates range from the end of the thirteenth to the middle of the seventeenth century; and each in his own fashion undoubtedly attained to the height of that form of mysticism which he practiced. Thanks to Prof. Ranade’s extensive knowledge and research, the voluminous writing of the five outstanding saints have been so sifted and garnered that the English-speaking public has in its hands a rare treasure.

Prof. Ranade leaves any comparison of these giants among the world’s mystics to his readers. He merely lays before us an account of progress, by the various ways of knowledge, faith, and works; and of the intense, widely differing, experience of the ultimate achievement. No student of mysticism can afford to ignore the Eastern types described in this book, illuminating as they do the spiritual life freed from all gross elements and utterly absorbed in reaching after the Divine.”

(The Journal of Theological Studies, Oxford, October, 1933)

49- Hugh I’Anson Fausset

An English writer, Literary Critic, Biographer and Poet

“The Maratha mystics, with whom professor Ranade is here concerned, represent only a section of the great mystical community in India, but it is a richly significant section. In the experience of every true mystic we find essentially the same drama enacted, the same path trodden, and the same revelation given……..  Yet, if we are to believe the testimony of these mystics, the Unitive Life of Spirit can only be attained by the road of extreme introversion.

Prof. Ranade claims for the Maratha Saints in general and for Jnanadeva in particular that they succeeded in reconciling Advaitism with Bhakti, Wisdom with Love, Monism with Pluralism.”

(The Aryan Path, November, 1933)

50- A. B. Latthe

The former Diwan of Kolhapur State

Professor of English, Rajaram College

“The reader of this volume must content himself, therefore, with a general history of the Bhakti school in Maharashtra which has been presented by Prof. Ranade with much care and after a deep study of the original writings of all the saint-poets who founded that school, and led it to be a great popular movement of religious as well as political awakening. As it is, the volume gives a coherent picture of the progressive development of Maratha thought.”

(The United India and Indian States, Delhi, June 17, 1933)

51- Prof. Mahendranath Sircar

Professor of Philosophy, Presidency College, Calcutta

“The book opens with a fine preface from the author in which he describes the nature of Mystical Experience, its ineffable, intuitive, and universal character.

Jnanadeva’s theory of the emergence of the world is called Sphurtivada, which maintains that the world is a sport of the one supreme intelligent Atman.

Namadeva presents the devotional type of Mysticism. He finds God’s Name as the most effective way God-realisation.

Ekanatha has put more emphasis upon love and devotion than upon intellectual knowledge. The author calls it Synthetic Mysticism.

The author then passes on to the personalistic mysticism of Tukarama.

The author finally gives an account of the Activistic Mysticism of Ramadasa, who with the heart set upon God directs all his energies towards the establishment of a kingdom of power and righteousness.

The book gives a beautiful bibliography of mystical writings: Indian, Islamic, Christian etc.”

(The Prabuddha Bharata, November 1933, pg. 565)

52- Prof. Alban G. Widgery

Duke University, USA

“I have already read a good deal of Prof. Ranade’s ‘Indian Mysticism’ and find it up to the excellent standard of Prof. Ranade’s work generally.”

(October 13, 1933)

53- Prof. G. S. Brett

“All genuine mysticism seems to be of one kind, and the differences are matters of expression, local colours, or personal taste….. The book is based on a careful study of sources, and those who pursue the study of mysticism, psychologically or otherwise, should not overlook this field.”

(The International Journal of Ethics)

54- A. D. Dhopeshwarker

“In the greater part of this volume he is working on untrodden ground, and yet the spirit of the original is so well preserved and the extracts are so well chosen and well arranged by him that we forget we are reading a translation. The whole of the inspiring literature will come as a revelation to those who do not know Marathi and will serve as a stimulus to the Marathi-knowing students to pay greater attention to their classics. We congratulate Prof. Ranade on these achievements.

In conclusion, we have only to express our profound reverence for the mystics and our unstinted admiration for the manner in which Prof. Ranade has executed his

difficult task of presenting their teachings to the public.”

(The Journal of the University of Bombay, Vol. I, part VI, May 1933)

55- The Asiatic Review, London

“A perfect monumental work! Professor Ranade has once more proved to be a great painstaking scholar as well as a deep philosopher.”

(July 1933)

56- A. Robertson

“The volume under review is a presentation in more or less a literal translation of the Source books published in Marathi, the extracts forming a garland of poems composed by the saints who cluster round the feet of Vithoba of Pandharpur from the 13th Century to the seventeenth, the first name among them, being Dnyaneshwara and the last Ramdas. The passages quoted are linked together and summarized in such away that the religious experiences of the various poets are systematically expounded……. To the whole is prefixed a short-essay on the mysticism in general with reference to some of the best known Christian mystics.

This book comes as a friendly challenge to Christianity. We suggest to all Christian Indians that the subject presented in this volume is of the utmost importance to them.”

(The National Christian Council Review, October 1933)

57- Nicol Macnicol

University of Glasgow, UK

“I earnestly hope that the book will attract wide-spread attention among those whom such religious experience interests and will reveal to many, who were not previously aware of it, the rich mines of such precious materials that are as yet largely unexplored and unknown to the West. India is extraordinarily rich in all kinds of religious experience and adventure, and to those whom these subjects attract, it has an endless interest. You are acting as an interpreter of this lore and I trust you will find many who will greatly value your service in this direction.

At last justice is being done on an adequate scale to the gifts of the Maratha         Saints, and being done by a scholar of their own kin.”

(Edinburgh, 1st November 1933)

58- Mr. J. B. Pratt

“The volume is the outcome of careful and extensive scholarship, involving not only intimate knowledge of Sanskrit and Marathi sources but also considerable acquaintance with the literature of Christian Mysticism. No small proportion of the value of the volume consists in presenting in English translation of many hymns and passages from these mystical writings which have hitherto been inaccessible in English.”

(The Philosophical Review, New York, November 1934)

59- Prof. Kokileswar Shastri

Professor of Philosophy &amp; Sanskrit, Calcutta University

“Prof. Ranade’s ‘Mysticism’ bears evidence of a remarkable amount of  labour and research on each page and deep scholarship and insight, not to say of an impartial and judicious treatment of the subject. The book will, I am sure,  prove an impetus to many an unbelieving and doubting soul, and deserves to be read and re-read in every house as a ‘Dharma-Grantha’ everyday, as people use the Gita, the Bhagavata and other works of that type.”

60- Prof. R. Naga Raja Sarma

Presidency College, Madras

“Let me sincerely and whole-heartedly felicitate Prof. Ranade on his fine and excellent exposition of the essentials of Maharashtra Mysticism which is second to none in the speculative history of humanity, and in the history of spiritual attempts at the practice of the Infinite and at coming face to face with the Infinite, the Supreme Maker.”

(The Review of Philosophy &amp; Religion, March 1933)

61- The Bombay Chronicle

“Prof. Ranade has done ample justice to his subject. From the first to the last page, the book bears the stamp of clear thinking, ripe scholarship, depth of understanding and a synthetic view of Philosophy and Religion. We are confident, that any one, who is interested in the search of ultimate Being, will be immensely benefited by going through this book.”

(Bombay, March 12, 1933)

62- The Statesman

“It has to be said that we have in this volume a masterly and scholarly review of the teachings and achievements of the great mystical school of philosopher saints….. and we have great reasons to be heartily grateful to the authors for this most useful work……. On the whole, the book maintains a very high standard throughout, and we can without reservation commend this volume to all students of

Indian philosophy.”                                                                                                            (Calcutta, May 28, 1933)

63- Commercial India

“The volume under review contains accounts of the lives and teachings of some such super-men who appeared and left their permanent impress on Maharashtra…… Incidently, it also deals with and describes others of a similar type belonging not only to other provinces of India, but to the different parts of the whole world. This is a most gratifying feature, for which one cannot but be grateful to the learned authors for their liberality and cosmopolitinaism.

The learned author has brought to bear a vast amount of erudition and painstaking research in writing the book, which we hope will be appreciated.”                                                                                                      (Calcutta, August 1933)

64- Prof. P. B. N.

“The author is himself a student of practical mysticism, and has studied the works of the Western mystics, and is thus most competent to deal with his subject. In his very important preface of thirty-five pages, he compares the work of the Western mystics with that of his countrymen.

We should recommend the volume to the attention of our educated public and shall await with pleasurable expectations the appearance of the next volume of this monumental series.”

(The Theosophist, Adyar, March 1933, Pg. 724)

65- K. K. Garde

“In this volume the author has given, in beautiful English, a substance of the inspired works of five great luminaries in the Indian spiritual firmament.

The titles given to the several mystics are very significant and apt, and the classification is remarkably original. It goes to show, in our opinion, that the writer is himself a mystic of the first type. In the illuminating preface of 35 pages are revealed the writer’s vast scholarship and intimate acquaintance with the works of a number of eminent philosophers and mystics of the East and West.

Prof. Ranade has rendered yeoman service to the cause of Indian Philosophy by this intelligent interpretation of our culture to the Western world and we are sure that the publication of this series would go a great way towards inspiring respect for our philosophy in the minds of the whole civilized world.”                                                                                                (The Hitvada*, Nagpur, August 27, 1933)

*English newspaper, founded by Gopal Krishna Gokhale in 1911

66- The Ramadas and Ramadasi

“The subject-matter of the Volume is an important one, and has come from the pen of a scholar with spiritual experience. This is the first work of the kind in English so far as Maharashtra literature is concerned, and beyond doubt it will be instrumental in adding to the reputation of Maharashtra in Europe and America. We heartily commend the easy style in which the author discusses even the difficult subject-matter of the work. Rarely shall we meet with anybody who would render the philosophical teaching of saints like Jnaneshwara so finely into English.

We had heard that the late Lokmanya Tilak had expressed the opinion that if there was any person more than another who could translate the ‘Gitarahasya’ into English, it was Professor Ranade. We now realise that the statement must be true.

Of supreme importance in the work is the Preface of 35 pages which forms the prologue of the book. …….It is an original composition of Prof. Ranade, and does not form part of the teaching of the Saints discussed in the book.

Prof. Ranade’s volume would be sufficient to give an inkling to our readers of the hundred Gitanjalis that exist in our spiritual literature. We have no doubt that Prof. Ranade’s work will tell the Western World to what heights of Philosophy, Ethics and Mysticism, the Maharashtra mystics had scaled. Prof. Ranade’s only intention is to find out the Truth, we entirely agree with the opinions he has expressed about the life and philosophy of Ramdas, his mysticism and its special characteristics.

The present work, nevertheless, is sufficient to whet our appetite, and we are sure that Prof. Ranade’s pen contains in it that which will appease it.”                  (Dhulia, September 2, 1933)

67- The Daily Dnyanprakash

“The mystical teaching of these saints in the matter of God-realisation required an expositor like Prof. Ranade, who had his mind attuned to the subject for a long time, and who was also skilled in Western philosophy, and it is a matter of great congratulation that the subject has ultimately found an author. There are very few people like Prof. Ranade in the whole of India, not to speak of Maharashtra itself, who can speak with an authoritative voice on such a subject after a comparative and critical study of Eastern and Western, Ancient and Modern, Philosophical and Mystical thought.

Prof. Ranade has served the cause of Maharashtra very well. He has a very commendable mastery over the English language, but we may also see from his work how deeply and critically he has studied the whole of the Bhakti literature of Maharashtra.”

(Pune, July 30, 1933)

68- S. S. William

“A great work! In his introduction the author traces the development of Indian Mysticism upto the age of Jnanadeva. He deals in a very interesting manner with the Mysticism of the Upanishads and the middle ages; of the Bhagavadgita…….

The author refers to the theory of the Christian influence on the Bhakti doctrine, both in the introduction and throughout the book. His conclusion on this debatable ground comes, in our opinion, nearest the truth…….He also says with jubilant hope that the world will await the day when the whole of the Jnaneshwari may be translated into English and thus be made available to the world of scholars. This creates in us a mood of curious expectancy.

It is with eager expectation that we await this new work in the hope that it will make clear this psychological and philosophical problem of mysticism in general that it will help to understand the Maratha mystics and the Tamil and other mystics, and that it will help people to walk on the Pathway to God.

(The servant of India, May 25, 1933)

69- The Hindustan Review, Patna

“…..The work under notice is one of great importance for scholars based on original sources, and heavily documented. We commend it to all students of Indian religion and philosophy….”

(Patna, April 1933)

70- The Vaitarani

“In India recently some attempts have been made to give to the West the richness of Indian ideas in a systematic way. But no author has dealt with them on such a vast scale, and tapped the main sources so exhaustively as the present authors have done. The authors are to be congratulated for this monumental work, which, we are sure, would change the wrong notions in the West about Indian Philosophy.”

(The Vaitarani Vol. VIII, No. I Chowdwar, Bihar and Orissa)
