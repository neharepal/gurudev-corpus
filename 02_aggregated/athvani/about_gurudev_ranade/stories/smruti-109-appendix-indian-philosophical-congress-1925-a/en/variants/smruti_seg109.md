2- Indian Philosophical Congress, 1925

Nobel Laureate               Governor of Bengal        Vice-Chancellor, Calcutta University

Rabindranath Tagore              The Earl of Lytton                        Sir Ewart Greaves

Indian Philosophical Congress came into existence in 1925. The credit for giving a shape to this idea must go to Prof. S. Radhakrishnan, Sir Brajendranath Seal and Dr. N. N. Sen. The Gordian knot was cut by selecting the great Rabindranath Tagore as the first President of the Congress. The opening of the Congress was done by the then Governor of Bengal, His Excellency The Earl of Lytton. It was a very heartening address to the philosophers assembled, for it came from one who had evidently read and digested a good few philosophical classics. In fact among the visitors it was a common remark: “We did not know that our Governor was a philosopher.”

The chairman of the reception committee and the then Vice-Chancellor Sir Ewart Greaves, welcomed the Congress and it’s invitees. Then followed the presidential address of Rabindranath Tagore. He read it in his musical voice with the emotion inseparable from a poet and he brought out the significance of philosophy in the life of India as he alone could have done. After the opening, the five different sections met: Logic and Metaphysics section presided over by The Rev. Dr. A. G. Hogg, Indian Philosophy section presided over by Prof. R. D. Ranade, History of Philosophy section presided over by Prof. Phanibushan Adhikary, Ethics section presided over by Prof. A. R. Wadia and Philosophy of Religion section presided over by Prof. G. H. Langley. The sectional presidential addresses were read to the sections concerned.

On the entertainment side all had a musical concert from the famous singer Shri Dilip Kumar Roy, who has gathered greater laurels since then. A visit to the Botanical Gardens with its famous banyan tree remains an unforgettable memory. At the end of the third day the session ended and the Congress has had no reason to look back.

(IPC: Silver Jubilee Commemoration Volume, 1950, Pg. 3)
