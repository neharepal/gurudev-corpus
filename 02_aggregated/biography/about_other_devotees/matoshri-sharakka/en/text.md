**Introduction**

Unwaveringly focus on your Divine Naam. Be steadfast in your devotion to your Guru (spiritual Teacher). The rest will be taken care of, by your guru", was the advice offered to everyone who was fortunate to meet Param Pujya Matoshri Sharakka of Dharwad, Karnataka State. And till her last breath, she remained steadfast to her advice.

Ajji to me and Matoshri Sharakka to others, was a disciple in the true sense of Shri Nimbargi Sampradaya, founded by the Saint Shri Nimbargi Maharaj of Devar Nimbargi, a small hamlet in Indi taluka located in Bijapur district of Karnataka State. It was his divine teachings and the supreme spiritual experience that paved way for many of his disciples to follow his path. His immediate disciple was Sadguru Shri Bhausaheb Maharaj of Umadi who later initiated both Sadguru Shri Amburao Maharaj (Baba of Jigajeevani) and Sadguru Prof. Shri R.D. Ranade (Gurudev of Nimbal) with Divine Naam. It was they who took forward the Sampradaya and spread the spiritual teachings. Matoshri Sharakka was initiated into the Sampradaya by Sadguru Shri Amburao Maharaj. After his niryana, it was Shri Gurudev Ranade who guided and led a young Sharakka whom he affectionately addressed as Sarakka, to the pinnacle of spiritual journey.

What separates our Sampradaya from others is that it is completely founded on the principles of Bhakti (devotional worship) and Guru. It's a Swarup Sampradaya', in which the pinnacle of meditation, God realization, is reached by Self-realization or Self-Vision'. The ultimate teaching of Shri Nimbargi Maharaj is, "We must dedicate our worldly life to Self and should forever be engaged in His meditation. Nothing ever happens without His will". He had also said that one can do this by following the routine worldly life and that there is no need to renounce the world or the family.

Param Pujya Matoshri Sharakka entirely adhered to this principle. A child widow, after her parents' demise, she not only looked after her own siblings, got them married but also helped them settle down. She was always present to help their children too to grow up well. She in fact was the most favourite aunt for all her nephews and nieces. With her affectionate smile, loving nature but mildly censuring now and then, she drew the entire family to her and from her to Shri Nimbargi Sampradaya. Every member of her large family including her grandnieces, grandnephews and now even great-grand nieces are disciples of the Sampradaya.


But in all these demanding family work, the one abandoned was her absolute dedication and concentration on the Divine Naam. She would quickly complete her household chores and hurry for meditation. She didn't wait to get a proper place to start meditation. Whichever small corner she could get, one would find her meditating. Day or night, heat or cold, physical discomfort or even the crowd around her didn't bother her. She just focused on meditation. Many a time Gurudev pointed out about her dedication to his other disciples. And whenever she could take leave from her family duties, she would rush to ShriKshetra Nimbargi and ShriKshetra Nimbal and rigorously follow her Guru's advice of meditation.

Sadhakas who asked her for practices to make time for meditation, she would laughingly say, "You shouldn't 'make' time for meditation. Meditation should be the main agenda. You should spare time for your regular work from meditation. Divine Naam and Nem (meditation) should be your center of focus. Daily chores should be done to help you live so that you can meditate more".

There are many such simple wisdoms from her that I share in this book. I her grandniece, was fortunate to have got an opportunity to spend my first eleven years of childhood with her. It was Matoshri Sharakka who brought me up, taught me to walk my first steps, first words, study, listen to stories from the epic Mahabharata and also taught all our bhajans by singing while putting me to sleep thus drawing me slowly to our Sampradaya. Even later, when I shifted to live with my parents-Sunanda, Dr Shrinivasrao Kadapa and my two younger brothers-Ravi and Shashi-- she would come to stay with us at least for a couple of months in a year and our school and college vacations were spent with her. My family of five was blessed to have her spend her last few years with us where we got an opportunity to meet all senior sadhakas and learn a lot more about Gurudev and our Sampradaya. We also realized that we were with a Living God. And in this process unknowingly I imbibed few of her teachings Even today, 36-years after her niryana, I seek her guidance and blessings by praying at her Samadhi in our Swananda home in Dharwad which was her last stop before she left on her next journey.

Through grandmother-granddaughter conversations using simple words, Param Pujya Matoshri Sharakka Ajji made me understand the importance of Guru and God. I have just put those conversations into text here. As we conversed in Kannada, I have written her advice to me in Kannada with translation in English in brackets.

**Preface**

When I received this slim book, authored by Smt. Surekha Kadapa-Bose, reminiscing about Param Pujya Matoshri Sharakka, I was delighted and started reading it with complete devotion and respect. As with any other book, I wanted to finish the entire book at one sitting. But I just couldn't. Having read the initial three or four incidents, I was so mesmerisingly immersed that I was transported to another world, the world of affection and complete devotion. The love, affection and reverence that I have for Param Pujya Shri Gurudev and Matoshri Sharakka increased many folds and I was carried to a different zone. A space from where I didn't feel like coming out. It was a universe of peace, tranquility and absolute veneration. I wanted to be in that state of mind for a longer time. So, I decided to enjoy that momentous emotion for a bit more time. And to prolong that experience of utter bliss for extended period and to continue to remain in that heavenly mood, I read the entire book in small parts till the end. I just thought of explaining to everyone the kind of impact this book of recollections had on me.

Though I had fleetingly met Param Pujya Sharakka earlier, an opportunity to actually meet and converse with her came in 1982, at the celebration of the 25th punyatithi anniversary of Param Pujya Shri Gurudev Ranade, at ShriKhestra Nimbal. I got an opportunity to talk, hear her voice, touch her feet, listen to her words and to closely experience a very affectionate, gentle, ever helpful, divinity of a woman saint and her world. I was also fortunate to get the best guidance to proceed on the path of spirituality. That is still the supreme and most important moment of my life. I was also privileged and blessed to get an opportunity to deliver a pravachan on her 25th punyatithi saptah at Swananda home in Dharwad.

Another momentous incident was when Param Pujya Sharakka appeared in my dreams. I can never forget that moment. She showered me with her blessings. That dream and her blessings are my most cherished instances of my life. Initially, it was a motherly love I felt towards her. As time passed,


I started looking at Matoshri Sharakka as one of the most important and senior sadhakas (seekers) of Nimbal. But when I started listening to other senior sadhakas talk about her greatness, her spirituality, their conversation with her, her guidance and her gentle demeanor, my respect turned into reverence. I realized that Matoshri Sharakka was indeed a great sakshatkari saint which confirmed my hitherto felt belief in her. I started understanding her rigorous meditation. unwavering Gurubhakti, flawless conduct, pious thoughts, ever loving helpful personality and several other spiritual qualities.

These reminiscences shouldn't be treated as just an informative book or to merely gain knowledge. We should accept this as a book of Param Pujya Matoshri Sharakka's real guidance to each of our individual journey in the world of spiritualism. As much as possible we should honestly try to understand and follow her ways of meditation, conduct and journey into the world of spiritualism. If we are able to comprehend and adopt even a few of these written steps, then reading this book will have a meaning.

The final words of Param Pujya Sharakka, "Physically my body may not be here, but my Swaroop will always be present at my place", should be humbly noted.

On behalf of every sadhaka, I am humbly and respectfully paying obeisance at the feet of Param Pujya Sharakka. I have only one request to her and that is to bless us and help us follow her path of spirituality as fast as possible. Entreatingly I ask my Sadguru to "Kadig hayisu Tande", to lead me by holding my hands till the end.

Smt. Surekha has gifted us with these very affectionate, loving and virtuous memories. These will immensely help future generation, will accurately guide them to increase their devotion. I offer my heartfelt appreciation for her efforts and pray at the feet of our Sadguru that her sincere reverential efforts will help her follow a successful spiritual path.

Dr Anilkumar Kulkarni, Mumbai

**W**hat I am attempting to write isn't a biography of Param Pujya Matoshri Sharakka. That has already been written in detail. This is my mother, late Smt. Sunanda Kadapa (Awwa), and I reminiscing about the most important and cherished person in our lives. Though her paternal aunt (Attya), Awwa always looked upon Matoshri Sharakka as her mother, so naturally for us, her three children-Ravi, Shashi and me-she was our only grandmother, Ajji. Because of our mother, we were also fortunate to get our spiritual Guru in Ajji. It was she who led us on the path of Shri Nimbargi Sampradaya and taught us to hold steadfastly on to the Naam (divine name of God bestowed specially by one's Guru).

Till her last breath, Awwa's advice to us, as she had received it from Ajji, was, "Hold the Naam tightly in your fist. Whatever adversaries you may face, don't let go of the Naam. Even if you are sinking in the flooding river, hold tight on to your Naam. Gurudev will rescue and help you".

With this golden teaching of Ajji, I will start writing with an incident that had happened around late 1950's as narrated to me by Awwa. It was the time of my brother Ravi's jawala (tonsuring) ceremony of a year-old-boy. My father, Dr S.N.Kadapa (Anna for us and for others Shinanna) was a Vaishnav Brahmin and all his relatives wanted the jawala ceremony of the first grandson of the Kadapa family to be conducted in ShriKshetra Mantralayam Math. It's the abode of Shri Raghavendra Swami and is located on the banks of the Tungabhadra River, in Kurnool district of Andhra Pradesh state. So, all the relatives along with two of us kids


decided to travel to Mantralayam which is around 300 kms away from our home town Dharwad, Karnataka state.

Awwa was very vague about how we travelled part journey, till we reached the point from where a ride on bullock cart was the only mode of transport available. In those days there were neither buses nor taxis and not even pucca road to reach the Math after one got off the train. So Ajji, Awwa, Anna and us two kids were in one bullock cart and the others in two more carts. The kuccha road was full of big pebbles and stones and the young driver of our cart with his young and healthy white bullocks pulling the cart, was very enthusiastic and driving it at full speed. The cart was swaying a lot and suddenly Ajji leaned across and held tightly on the enclosing high wooden frame support of the cart above the wheel. of the

Anna thought she was getting scared and told her that he would ask the driver to slow down. But the energetic bullocks were not willing to slow down as sometimes it's very difficult to control bullocks and horses when they are charged up and galloping. Ajji, E with complete concentration and a soft smile looked at Anna and said, "Kalaji maad byada Shinanna. Na hidadini (Don't worry Shinanna. I am holding)." Anna didn't understand it then and was wondering why his relative following behind were calling out to him.

When we alighted at the entrance of the Math, many people came up to us and asked how did we travel so safely when one of the wheels didn't have the long key that attaches it to the cart. On their arrival, a couple minutes later, Anna's relatives informed them that the fulcrum long key of the wheel had come off at least a couple of miles back. They couldn't understand how the cart hadn't toppled, as the speed at which the cart was travelling on that treacherous road, would have certainly resulted in a serious accident.

"This is when we realised that it was your Ajji holding the cart


and keeping us safe. But as usual Attya never took any credit but gently told us, "Nann Guru shreshta iddara. Awarige namm kalaji ada. My Guru is great. He takes care of our well-being)." This was the turning point in our lives. Till then I loved her as my mother and never as my aunt but after this incident both your father and I began to understand her greatness and started revering her," narrated Awwa with tears profusely flowing down her cheeks, as she reminisced about her Attya, who was her biggest supporter and mentor. This incident also drew my scientist father to our Shri Nimbargi Sampradaya.

**T**oday when I look back, I think the best thing that ever T happened to me was spending the first ten to eleven years of my life with Ajji. Anna was an agricultural scientist and was always posted at government agricultural research farms in small villages, which in the 1950's, were almost cut off from any amenities like school, hospitals etc. And according to Awwa, my brother Ravi wasn't a very strong child when he was born and needed constant attention. As she found it difficult to look after two young kids, Ajji offered to look after me.

That was the best gift from my mother to me. Even Indira Ajji, Awwa's mother had left her in the care of Ajji. Awwa, the eldest daughter, was barely 12 years old when her mother passed away. Worried about her as she too, like Ravi was a delicate child, Indira Ajji, had asked Matoshri Sharakka saying, "Vainasa, Sunandan nimm kaiyyaag kottini, naa hod myala, niva akin nodikollari (Sister-in-law, I am leaving Sunanda in your care. After my demise, please take care of her)." Ajji quietly looked at her for a few seconds and said, "Nann kaiyyag kotii houdallo. Inn aaki nannaki. Nee kalaji maad byada (You are giving her to me, right? Now she is mine. Don't worry about her)."

This was in 1940's and Ajji not only looked after Awwa but


even my father and us three siblings. And when Awwa was in distress she did the same thing and entrusted me to Ajji. She told me many times, "I left you because Attya was there to take care of you". That was such a blessing in disguise which resulted in me thinking that Ajji is mine and I belong to her and my parents were outsiders! Though my mother would come often to her maternal home I still believed that I belonged to Ajji.

Unknowingly, those ten to eleven years of my life paved the way for my present unwavering trust, reverence, and love for ShriKshetra Nimbal and Param Pujya Shri Gurudev Ranade, as I always accompanied Ajji on her visits to Nimbal. At least four to five times in a year for a short trip of two to three days, we would go there. I started treating those days and the journey as picnic and vacation time. The best thing I loved was the prasaad time after aarati and bhajan I got to eat Ajji's share of bhendu and bhattas (dry sweets made only with sugar) too.

In Dharwad I grew up in my maternal grandfather, Ajja, the renowned Hindustani vocalist Pandit Gururao Deshpande's home located on Ghali Oni in Mangalwar Peth. After the demise of his wife Indira Ajji, it was Matoshri Sharakka who took care of his four children till they all completed education, got employed and married. He too was an ardent disciple of Sadguru Gurudev Ranade and had performed many times in Param Pujya Gurudev's lecture series. Ajji was his younger sister. It was she who had requested Param Pujya Gurudev to bless her brother with the Divine Naam.

**A**ll my childhood memories are with Ajji. She taught me basic maths, tables up to 30 and English alphabets. I remember this incident very clearly as it was a daily routine. In Ghali Oni, every house had kattas (stone platforms) out of both the sides of the front door. Even our house


had two-a broader one to the left of the door and a narrow one on the right side. And there was a street lamp at the end of the right katta. Every night after dinner, Ajji would sit on the broader katta, make me sleep on her lap and sing the ratri bhajans. I learnt my Baara Abhang composed by the great Saint Tukaram of Maharashtra, which is the major part of our ratri bhajan, from her singing. It was a pleasant and most soothing lullaby a child could listen to. She would cover me with her red sari pallu and make me fall asleep. In those days, Ajji draped red mulmul nine-yard saris. In her later years she switched to white mulmul saris.

Listening to her singing, I would playfully peep up through her red sari pallu at the night street lamp and see myriad brilliant, coloured stars, many of them. Enjoying looking at so many of them, I would fall asleep. This continued for as long as I can remember.

A child's curiosity has no boundaries. I must have been five or six years old when one day, as Ajji was a couple of minutes late to finish post-dinner work, I went to the katta, lifted my cotton mulmul petticoat to peep at the lamp's light. All I could see was plain pale-yellow bulb light. Not to be defeated, I climbed on the katta, slept in nearly the same position as I would while on her lap, tried to see the light. Still, I saw only the plain yellow bulb.

Ajji came out and saw my antics. Smilingly she asked me what was I trying to do! The innocent young me angrily asked her why do I see the beautiful stars only through her sari pallu and not through my petticoat? "Nee yen jaadu madati yen? (Do you do some magic?)" I asked her.

Laughing, she picked me up, put me on her lap and without any explanation started singing the bhajans. She only said, "Nanag tada aat. Nee mallako (I am late. You sleep)." Initially, fearing I might not see the beautiful lights, I closed my eyes tight and refused to open my eyes to look at the lamp from inside her sari pallu. But


then the curiosity overtook and I opened my eyes. And once again I was aghast looking at the sight of those brilliant stars.

I wanted to ask her the reason but she was completely immersed in her bhajans and I didn't have the courage to disturb her. Next day morning before I could ask her, she told me, "Ne innu sannaki iddi. Ninag tilyudilla. Summan nodukot eeru (You are still very young. You won't understand. Quietly see and enjoy)."

I really wish I had never grown up and could continue to sleep in her lap and look at the brilliant stars! No telescope can show you the brilliance of those stars.

**A**nother important episode I remember was Ajja's music lessons with his students. At our home there was always the sound of music, as Ajja had many shishyas. Morning and evening times were the music teaching and riyaaz time. I am the first and only trained Bharat Natyam dancer in our family. My mother and both aunts (Mala Dixit and Vijaya Lingsur) are vocalists. The varied bhav (expressions) I could emote while dancing, for which I got the first prize at the Mysore State Sangeet Natak Academy in junior category competition, was because of both the grandparents who would correct and guide me, expressing the words of philosopher-saint Purandara Dasa's padas or compositions.

The reason why I am writing about the episode is because through this, Ajji taught and told me about the importance of Guru and God. This is an incident etched sharply in my memory. Ajja was teaching the song Aatan Paduve anavarata to Ratnaprabha Joshi Moushi, one of his shishya (student). And she wasn't getting the expressions right. Ajja called out to me and asked me to dance. Ever ready to dance, I tied my ghungroos and waited for him to begin singing. I was perplexed when he asked me to perform on a new song which I had never heard before. It was late-morning time on a Sunday.


Ajji sitting at the door leading to the kitchen from the drawing room and churning buttermilk, smiled gently at me and said, "Naa ninag artha belatini, nee madu! (I will tell you the meaning and you dance!)." Thus began our interactive programme for more than half an hour or so. Ajja would sing those three words and Ajji would tell me, "Devar besuru yavaggiddaru togotini (I take God Almighty's name at all times of my life)." On every daily routine like eating, playing, cooking, reading, washing vessels-clothes, at home, talking with friends, plucking flowers, making garland, sleeping and so on. She would tell me each occasion and after taking a second to understand it, I would perform and Ajja would sing again. We all started enjoying this interaction. At one point when Ajji stopped, I asked her, "Ajji nee benni kadilikatti, eegu Devar besuru togalikkatti? (Ajji are you taking God's name even now when you are churning the butter?)." Smilingly she said, "Hoon (Yes)." And I performed that too.

After it ended, I was feeling very proud and important as everyone appreciated it a lot. The icing on the cake was Ajji giving me the freshly churned butter to eat. Till that moment, for some reason I had never liked eating butter, may be because I found it too soft and slippery. I loved the ghee but never the butter. Ajji knew it. But in the euphoria of the appreciation that I had received, when she put a dollop of butter in my small hands, I ate it and was surprised at the sweet taste. The cheeky young kid that I was, I articulated, "Aree, yee benni yestu ruchi ada! (This butter is so tasty!)." Gently smilingly but with all seriousness, she said, "Idu Devar benni. Mari byada (This is the butter from God. Don't forget it!)."

That was my first introduction to God by my Ajji.

**S**oon after this, I remember accompanying Ajji to a jewellery shop on the main market in Dharwad. I always tagged along with her wherever she went. Her outings were very rare.


And I think the jewellery shop that I am referring to was the earlie shop of Mahindrakar's jewellery shop located on Subhash Road, next to the Guledagud shop. I was really young because the nine to ten small steps to climb up to the shop was a novelty to me. It was fun going up and down to play till Ajji was inside the shop. In later years the Dharwad Municipality raised the height of the footpath and the road, so now there are just four or five steps to reach those rows of shops on the road and the jewellery shop too has shifted to another location.

Ajji was getting silver padukas made. I didn't know anything about this then. I only got to know about it when we went back to the shop after some days to get them. I was highly intrigued by the tiny, shining, beautiful padukas and thought she had got them for me to play!! The next day our family priest, Dattam Bhatt who was also a tabla player and accompanied Ajja on many occasions, came home to perform a puja. It was the paduka sthapana puja. I didn't understand the meaning then.

The house was decorated with a festival look. The outer door frame was adorned with lot of mango leaves toran, large rangoli was drawn on the front porch and of course Ajji had prepared holigi and payasa (puran poli and kheer), along with other dishes. As I was playing outside, one of my neighbours, Krishnabai Ajji, asked me what the occasion was. Now when I look back, I realise that as a kid, I was really cheeky. I told her, *"Ajji Gurundu chappal puja ada!* (There is puja of Ajji's Guru's chappal!)."

Krishnabai Ajji knew about my Ajji and her spirituality. She got angry at my explanation and later told Ajji about how disrespectfully I had spoken of the event. I got scared that Ajji would get angry. She didn't. All she said was, *"Sannaki iddal. Innu tiliyudilla!* (She is young, doesn't yet understand things)." She took me inside, asked me to wash my hands and feet and took me in front of the small alcove she had of her own in the kitchen where


she kept her own puja items, angaar (holy ash) and photographs of Shri Gurudev and now the *padukas.*

*"Eevaru ninn Gurudevaru iddar. Eevaru devaru. Nee eeverige deenalu namaskar madi avaran priti le nenesidar, ninaga yendu traas agudilla.* (He is your Guru also. He is God. Every day, if you bow before Him and recall Him with love, you won't have any problems)." And pointing to the padukas she said, "Eevu Gurudevaradu. Adakka Paduka antar. Deenalu, munjane, nee padukad mele baggi namaskar madu. Gurudevarige muttada (These are Shri Gurudev's. They are known as padukas. Every morning, bow before these padukas. Your namaskar will reach Shri Gurudev)."

I bowed down obediently but as I said the brash kid I was, I had to ask, "Nanagu evu beku. Nanag yavag kodatidi? (I too want them. When will you give them to me?)" For a minute she was quiet and was looking at the padukas. A minute to two for an impatient kid is a long time to bear. So, as I tugged at her hands, she looked down at me and said, "Naa bodmyale evu ninnu. (After I leave, they will be yours)." I was shocked at her reply and instantly started crying, "Nee nanna bittu yellu bog byada. Naa paduka bedodilla. (Don't leave me and go anywhere. I won't ask for the padukas)."

Those silver padukas even today are kept next to Ajji's Samadhi at the feet of Gurudev's photograph in the house of my parents' at Swananda in Dharwad. They will remain there forever.

This is how she started initiating me into our Sampradaya, without forcing but softly talking about her Guru with her own gentle behaviour. It was in this tender way she spoke to anyone who came home or wherever she went, that had a deep and everlasting impact on me. She always said, "Surekha, *yaar manige nav bogativi, aur manas noyisa byadadu, matt namm manige yaar baratar aur manas noyisa byadadu. Avaran khushi khushi kalasbeku.* (Surekha, never hurt anyone whom you go to visit and never hurt any who come visiting your home. They should always

a lot of microscope work. And then out of the blue he asked if I could bend backwards. I said as I was a Bharat Natyam dancer, I could touch the floor bending back and showed him by performing. He looked at my father with a smile and told him and Sureshkaka that may be all the four specialist doctors we had consulted in Dharwad-Hubballi had made a mistake or some miracle had happened. "Your daughter has a posture problem which will be taken care by ten-days of ultrasound treatment and some mild exercises," he remarked.

We just couldn't believe what we had heard. Thanking the doctor profusely, we left.

The serene and calm expression that Ajji had, when the next day Anna and I informed her after reaching Pune had to be seen to believe. She, along with Krishnarao Ajja, was meditating when we reached home. She looked at our enthusiasm and quietly said, "Let's do arati". Though Anna left the same night, I stayed back to get my treatment at Sancheti Hospital in Pune. Ten days was all it took for the fever and pain of more than six months to vanish. I was as good as new. As the doctor said it was a miracle.

**T**he next incident I am narrating is when I stayed with T Madhumama and Sudhamami Kumathekar at their home for a year in Jogeshwari, Mumbai. Courtesy Madhumama I was working as a senior teacher in a CBSE school in Navy Nagar, next to Colaba. I stayed as their eldest daughter and like her three children-Milind (Bhau), Makarand and Meera-I too addressed her as 'Aayi'. That year I learnt the hard life a Mumabikar leads which prepared me for my future. This was in 1981.

My routine was to leave home by 7 a.m, take a local train to Churchgate and then bus to reach Navy Nagar and return home the same way by 6 p.m. The entire time I rarely drank water. New city, new people and I found it difficult to adjust and so my body reacted.


By December I fell sick and diagnosed a severe case of UTI (urinary tract infection) as I rarely drank water. Fever, shivering and body pain. On the third day the fever reached 104.5 and both Aayi and Mama panicked. Though the family doctor had started me on antibiotics and Aayi was putting cold compressors on my f forehead, the fever refused to budge. And the back pain was so severe that I was crying with the pain and Aayi couldn't sleep. She was sitting massaging my back and putting cold water compressor on my forehead. Post mid-night I told Aayi that in the cupboard where I kept my clothes there was a small packet of angaar that Ajji had given and requested her to bring and apply it on my back. She brought it and applied observing that my body was unbearably hot and told Madhu Mama that in the morning they should admit me in the hospital. He agreed.

This conversation took place at around 1 in the middle of the night and I asked her to go and get some sleep. A couple of hours later when she came to check my fever, she got scared that I was not responding to her. She shook me awake and found that there was almost no fever and I was in deep painless sleep. Thermometer showed the fever to be around 99.8. And next day by 6 in the morning as usual I got up to prepare to go to work as I had already missed three days. And no one looking at me could have said that I was sick for three days and had 104.5 fever in the night. But of course, both Mama and Aayi forced me to rest that day.

Madhumama kept the angaar packet to himself and told me, "You have Ajji. What more do you need! It was her blessings that saved you your love and trust in her the way you did last night. She will earlier with your backpain and even today. You continue protect you!".

I am sure there are many old and young sadhakas who have been There are many other incidents where she has protected me, and lucky to get her blessings. Once she had said, "Trass yellarigu baratav.


*Kelvandu namm prarbdha matt kelvandu namm vyavahar. Aadar Gurunn myala puran preeti, vishws madidar avaru namm trass benni olgandi kudala tagandhang tagitaar* (Every human being faces problems. Some problems arise due to our past deeds and some others due to our behaviour. But if we implicitly trust our Guru, he takes away our problems without even our knowledge like removing a hair from soft butter)."

you

Continuing, she added that a Shrestha Guru, takes care of our past and future. A Guru never abandons his disciple. Even if forget him in your worldly pursuits, he brings you back to the right path. It's very difficult to get such a Guru. A person has to be blessed by God to find such a divine Guru. And she was my GURU.

**I**n one of her last conversations that Awwa had with Ajji, in the month of March in 1986, just a few weeks before her demise, Awwa had asked her as to whom shoulde she approach for help after her niryana. Ajji calming a grieving Awwa had said, "I will always be here. Our swaroop will always be here. Where will that go? Physically I won't be here to talk to you daily. But the swaroop will never leave this place. You or anyone can come to this house and this room, meditate with full faith and their wishes will be fulfilled. Why are some places called as punyakshetras? Because the swaroop of satpurush is there. Temples and holy places are different from punyakshetras."

And because of this statement of Ajji, my Awwa stayed alone in that house for nearly 35 years after Ajji's niryana. Even when she came to stay with us in Pune and Mumbai, she always hurried to go back to Swananda in Dharwad saying, "My Attya is waiting for me there!"

I will tell you about my own belief in Ajji's Swaroop always being present to guide and protect us. Earlier, before taking any major decision we always asked Ajji's consent. She would say that she


would inform us the next day. After meditating the entire night, gave us her advice the next morning. And we all followed it. This was not only of our family even other sadhakas would travel all the way to Dharwad and seek her advice/permission. I remember Jeurkar Shashtrimama, Shyamrao Kumthekarmama, Prahlad Kulkarnimama and many others sought her blessings and followed her advice.

After her niryana, I recalled her assurance to Awwa that her Swaroop will always be there, and one can seek her blessings. At the time of my marriage to Hiren, I was in a dilemma and a bit worried about my decision to marry a Bengali. Recalling Ajji's words, I travelled down from Mumbai to Dharwad, made two small paper chits. On one I wrote 'Marry Hiren' and on the other 'Don't marry Hiren'. Folded the chits neatly and kept them on Ajji's samadhi at our home in Swananda. Awwa was a bit concerned. She warned me that if I took this step, I will have to abide by whatever the chit revealed. I agreed.

Both Awwa and I meditated for an hour or so. Then did arati. Before lighting the arati, Awwa warned me again and said to rethink. After I agreed to abide by Ajji's decision, we lit the arati. And then without hesitation or second thought, I picked up one chit on any which was her permission to marry Hiren. Now it's been 35 years and we are still going strong. Not only that, though a staunch leftist and an atheist, Ajji slowly and steadily converted Hiren into becoming a disciple of our Sampradaya. Today before taking any major decision, even he asks me to seek Ajji's permission.

There are many such incidents in our lives after Ajji. I will narrate just one more. My elder daughter, Arunabha (Abha) was at crossroads after her 12th exams. She was unsure of what stream of studies she should opt for. She was very good in biology, hated maths but loved English. Having two successful engineer brothers, I was toying between engineering and medicine line. With the high


percentage of marks, she had scored at her 12th and then the CET, she would have got admission in either of these streams. Her love for English literature especially, as she had joined St. Xavier's College in Mumbai which is famous for producing many successful in that subject, was hindering our decision. So naturally Ajji had to rescue me and my daughter from this dilemma. persons

I came down to Dharwad, bowed before her samadhi and asked for her decision. I prepared four small chits one each for one subject-medicine, engineering, English and basic science. Writing basic science was an afterthought as pursuing a degree in basic science is not much sought after even today. I am talking about the year 2006. After doing the Nem and lighting arati, I picked up a chit. And it read 'Basic science'. I was shocked and so was Awwa. She asked me if I still wanted to adhere to Ajji's decision. I said 'yes' but also said that the final decision will be Abha's. My daughter without hesitation accepted it very happily and today she holds a Ph.D. in biochemistry, has done post doctoral research for two years at the prestigious IGC Institute in Lisbon (Portugal) and now based in Lisbon is working as a Scientific Managing Editor for one of the best science publishing houses in the world. She is going places. Look at the far sightedness of Ajji's advice to me and my daughter. As she used to say, "All we have to do is trust our Guru and the rest will be taken care of!"

**L**astly, I want to write about her last message to me. I recall the day when Shakutai Maushi (the only daughter of Shri Gurudev), had come to Dharwad to pay homage and participate on the 13th day ceremony after Ajji's niryana. She asked and advised me, "So Sharakka told you about her decision of niryana? You are really lucky that you were the chosen one by Matoshri Sharakka. Never forget the dream and always


remember her words." Others like Ramanna Kulkarnimama, Pralhadmama, Kumthekarmama, Masalekarmama, Ramdurgkarmama and several others who were standing behind her in Ajji's room, also said the same thing. Though none of them are alive today from that group of sadhakas, I wish I could tell each of them that I haven't forgotten the dream and hopefully I never will. Even today 36 years after Ajji left us, I still remember the incident as though it had happened only last night.

I was living in the YWCA hostel in Colaba in Mumbai and working as a journalist with Free Press Journal at Nariman Point. I shared my room with another girl. It was very early morning, at around 3 on Friday 21st March 1986. Ajji appeared in my dream. She woke me up and asked, "Surekha, Nann nalku Gurugol nann mund nintar mat nann karilikattar. Naa en madali? (Surekha all my four Gurus are standing in front of me and asking me to come. What should I do?)" Without a second thought I said, "Ajji naa aram eerutteni, nee bogu (Ajji I will be alright, you go)."

Sadhakas who have known our family will remember that we were going through a very bad patch in those years. I won't go in detail about that. And Ajji was holding our family tight. The moment in my dream I said, "Nee bogu (you go)." I woke up sobbing loudly with tears flowing profusely and started thinking of what that dream was all about. In those days we didn't have a phone at home in Dharwad. So, there was no way of my knowing exactly what was happening there. My sobbing woke up my roommate and she tried to console me. Thinking it must be just a bad dream next day morning I went to my office feeling some kind of premonition as I knew that when a satpurush appears in our dreams and says something, the dream has to come true. But I just didn't want to believe this dream. I had the same dream the next night too and my response was the same. Next day being Sunday, my roommate


decided to take me out to trek at Borivali National Park to keep me busy and distracted.

I wanted to leave immediately for Dharwad but Anna coming to Mumbai as he was receiving an award from Indo American Hybrid Seeds (India), on Tuesday, so I stayed back. When I met him at the Dadar station and told him about my dream, his first response was, "Forget the award. Let's leave immediately". But then he recollected what Ajji had told him as he was leaving for Mumbai. "Shinanna, naa baadi nodteni. Nee ninn award togund lugu baa (Shinanna, I will be waiting for you, you return soon with the award)." So, we stayed back. was

It was then that he told me that Ajji's health had deteriorated a lot, the doctor had stopped the medicines and injections from Friday, the day she first appeared in my dream. But Anna was sure that nothing would happen, "Otherwise I wouldn't have come," he consoled me.

On Monday night Ajji once again appeared. This time she was lying down on the cot in her room and looked very frail. There were many sadhakas sitting in the room. She looked at me and said, "Send everyone out." After everyone went out, she asked me to close the door and light arati. She asked me to sing the arati loudly, Namo namo yembe Guruve kadig baisu tande... I did as she asked me. In between singing the arati I was peeping at her. With complete concentration she was focussed on the arati. At the end of the second time when we sing Jai Guru, Jai Guru, Jai Guru Jai... I saw Ajji's image in the flame of arati. Shocked, I turned to look at her and found that Ajji has left me..

Somehow Anna and I managed to stay calm and collect the award but didn't wait to have the dinner hosted for him for the award. He apologised and gave a very brief acceptance speech and said, "My mother is very unwell. I will have to leave." We reached Dharwad early morning of Wednesday by Southern


Travel bus and reached home around 8. I saw that there were many people who had already come home. Our relatives and sadhakas. From the front gate I ran to see her and with closed eyes Ajji was lying on the cot with many sitting around her. She acknowledged my presence with a very mild smile and Anna showed her his award certificate. Though she couldn't open her eyes, she just smiled.

I couldn't control and went to the back of our house and sat on the katta for washing clothes started crying. Umakaku (Tendulkar) came up behind: I me and tried to console me saying, "Surekha, kalaji karu nakos. Tya bare honar (Don't worry, Surkeha, she will be alright)." That's when I narrated to her my dream. Even Kaku was shocked and started crying. She went inside the house and told everyone about it. Every member was in shell shock as everyone still had some hope, which was dashed after listening to my dream. They all immediately left for the Post Office near the railway station, to book trunk calls for all their relatives and sadhakas living in Nimbal, Pune, Sholapur, Mumbai, Jamakhandi, Bengaluru, Bijapur, Nasik and every other place. With whatever mode of transport, they could get, sadhakas started reaching our home, Swananda. By night our house was over flowing with people sitting inside, outside, on the steps leading to terrace, on the terrace itself. More than 250 people had reached.

Around 8pm on the night of 26th, Wednesday, Anna summoned our family physician who gave us the sad news that even morning might be too far. Accepting the inevitable, I told Awwa that we should start reading Pothi, without fail do raatri bhajan and continue to take turns to read the Dasbodh throughout the night.

This wasn't my thought at all. It was completely Ajji's. It was she

who during one of our innumerable evening talks had told me that when Param Pujya Gurudev was reaching his end on this Earth, this is what the sadhakas in ShriKshetra Nimbal had done. "That's the best homage one can pay to a *satpurush*. S/he will love being


surrounded by the ambience of meditation, the still calmness of Divine *Naam* till the end and not get disturbed in final meditation. No one should trouble by crying loudly or making a scene at these last moments on the earth," was Ajji's advice to me. In retrospect, I now feel she was preparing me as to what we should do when the time came for her departure from this world.

Throughout the night the only sound we could hear was the voice of the person who was reading the Dasbodh. We all took turns to read one chapter and no one even thought of sleeping even for a second. Around 2 in the morning Ajji started feeling breathless but she was still calm. As I was sitting on the floor near her cot softly massaging her hands, I could hear her rhythmic breathing, with eyes partially opened, nasikagra drishti, even till the last stage.

It was at 2.22 a.m. on Thursday the 27th March, on Tukaram Beej, when she took her last breath and left us.
